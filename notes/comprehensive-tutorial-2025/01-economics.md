# Economics — Comprehensive Tutorial 2025 (Tarkusarkusar)
**Source:** [The Comprehensive Victoria 3 Tutorial (2025) | Iberian Twilight](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P_PzglCFo9I) by Tarkusarkusar, 2025-12-16 — chapter "1. Economics" [04:50].

## Summary
Victoria 3's economy is a loop: buildings produce goods, pops buy goods, profits pay wages, wages fund more buying — and in the early game you tax that loop through wages, not profits. A high Standard of Living matters because richer pops buy more, raising building profits [05:30]. Peasants are an untapped resource to be converted into productive workers by building [10:32], and once domestic demand is exhausted you grow further via trade [11:30], specialization, and companies, which give large throughput bonuses and cheaper construction [12:31].

## Core mechanics
1. **The economic loop is the heart of the game.** Buildings produce goods sold to other buildings or to pops; because pops buy things, buildings earn profits, which pay wages, which pops use to buy more goods. At the most basic level buildings exist to make profits and pay dividends to owners [06:00].
2. **Why keep Standard of Living high:** pops with higher SoL purchase more goods, which raises the profits on buildings — Tarkus calls this "the beating heart" of the economy [06:00].
3. **Not all money is equally useful to you; high profit is not the same as usefulness.** The most common new-player pitfall is reading high profits as a measure of a building's usefulness — what matters is which forms of money you can actually use [06:31].
4. **Where building income goes depends on ownership** [06:31]–[07:01]:
   - Capitalist-owned: part to the capitalist's pocket (spent on goods → other industries' profits), part to the Investment Pool.
   - Government-owned: a portion goes straight into the government budget, the rest is destroyed for "government inefficiency."
   - Worker-owned: money goes to workers' pockets, and they do NOT use the Investment Pool unless you have the Cooperative Ownership law (a late-game concern).
5. **Early game, you tax businesses via the wages they pay, not via dividends.** Until dividend taxes unlock later, the way to raise tax revenue early is to have higher wages [07:01].
6. **Wages depend on job quality and state SoL.** Wages are based on the average Standard of Living in a state, but more importantly on the job: better jobs (engineers, machinists) pay more, and better Production Methods / buildings create those better jobs [07:30].
7. **Profits still matter even when you can't tax them** because they feed the Investment Pool — the money the private sector ("private queue") has for building. Richer, more profitable buildings contribute more to the pool, which builds more buildings, which pay more taxable wages [07:30]–[08:01].
8. **Peasants are an untapped resource, not just "bad."** They live off subsistence and barely participate in the economy [08:30]. Their money comes from "subsistence output" — money that doesn't really exist, just the game letting them buy "fake" goods [09:01]. They pay few taxes, buy few goods, and supply few goods to the market [10:32].
9. **Peasants depress wages.** They tend to have low SoL, which drags down the state's average SoL and therefore depresses wages in that state [10:32].
10. **Convert peasants by building.** Peasants will happily take better jobs as soon as buildings make them available; while peasants exist you won't struggle to staff buildings [11:00].
11. **Running out of peasants (economy stalling) is a good sign** — it means you've succeeded at employing them. When you stall, don't give up; grow via trade [11:00]–[11:30].
12. **Trade's main purpose is creating demand without new employment.** Beyond accessing goods you can't produce, trade creates demand for goods without requiring new labor — a trade center employs only ~1,000 pops vs ~5,000 for most buildings, so you can adjust good prices without consuming much labor [12:31].
13. **Companies = specialization.** A company is a group that owns particular industries (e.g. Belgium's John Cockerill owns tools, steel, motors). Companies are extremely efficient: they add large throughput to the buildings they own [13:00]–[14:03].
14. **Throughput defined:** an increase to BOTH the input goods consumed and the output goods produced by a building — but it does NOT increase wages [14:03]–[14:31].
15. **Throughput matters for construction efficiency:** for the same Construction Points spent, a higher-throughput building creates more economic output per Construction Point and per worker [14:31]–[15:02].
16. **Companies are doubly efficient:** they add throughput to buildings, AND when they build via the private queue their construction efficiency is higher than yours, so they spend fewer Construction Points [15:02].
17. **Specialization + trade combo:** a company controlling an industry, kept profitable via trade, yields more goods and profits from the same workers and Construction Points [15:30].
18. **Risk of specialization: flooding the market.** Spamming one good can crash its price; trade fixes this by creating extra demand so prices stay high enough to keep buildings profitable. Reliance on trade scales inversely with market size — tiny Belgium floods the iron market fast; a Britain market (India, Canada, British Isles) takes far longer to saturate [15:30]–[16:00].
19. **Company charters** [16:30]–[17:02]:
   - Investment charters: let companies build HQs in nations where you have investment rights (advanced).
   - Trade rights: let companies own trade centers, making them more efficient.
   - Colonization rights: a company colonizes a state (usually Africa) and forms a new nation, like the East India Company.
   - Industry rights: grant a company access to another (predefined) industry.
   - Monopolies: no one else can build/purchase that industry type, and prices on its goods rise (monopolies raise all prices).
20. **Prestige goods** [18:00]–[18:30]: some companies produce them; they can be renamed for flavor. Mechanically they increase throughput on buildings that use them as an input, give more trade advantage when traded, raise SoL for pops that consume them (if consumable), and (for military prestige goods) boost army stats.
21. **Tariffs and subventions are for guiding trade, not for income** [19:31]–[20:00]. Tariffs add taxes to trades (give budget money but shrink trade volume); subventions are the inverse (pay a bonus per trade of a good to encourage more trade, costing budget). Their real purpose is steering trade and manipulating prices, not generating budget money.

## Numbers & formulas
- A company-owned tooling workshop gets **+15% throughput** because it's owned by a company [14:03].
- Example: a tooling workshop costs **600 Construction Points**; with **30 Construction Points** of capacity it takes **20 weeks** to build [14:31].
- Throughput example: **5% throughput** = 5% more input demand and 5% more output supply for the same 600 Construction Points [14:31]–[15:02].
- A trade center employs only about **1,000 pops**, vs a baseline of about **5,000 pops** for most buildings [12:31].
- Peasant non-participation example: a Wonian peasant pop of **600,000 people** consumes only **6 grain** out of **516 grain** in the market [09:30]–[10:01].

## Common pitfalls
- **Treating high profit as proof a building is useful.** Profits are nice, but think about which money is actually usable (taxable wages vs Investment Pool) — this is the most common new-player mistake [06:31].
- **Leaving peasants in place.** They depress state SoL and wages, pay little tax, and barely trade — convert them by building [10:32].
- **Over-specializing a small economy.** Spamming one good in a tiny market crashes its price; you need trade to absorb the surplus [15:30]–[16:00].
- **Treating tariffs/subventions as a revenue tool.** Untariffed trade's extra profits usually beat tariff income; tariffs/subventions are for guiding trade and prices [20:00]. New players can largely ignore them, or experiment to learn them [19:31]–[20:00].
- **Over-thinking trade advantage as a new player.** Trade advantage matters, but making a lot of a good matters more; just mouse over the trade-center number if curious [18:30]–[19:00].

## Reusable lessons / decision rules
- Keep Standard of Living up because rich pops buy more, which raises building profits [06:00].
- In the early game, grow tax revenue through **wages**, not dividends — build better buildings/PMs to create higher-paying jobs [07:01], [07:30].
- Value profits even when you can't tax them: they fund the Investment Pool that builds more taxable jobs [08:01].
- Treat peasants as fuel: build to employ them; an economy that stalls from running out of peasants means you did it right [11:00].
- When domestic demand is exhausted, grow via **trade** — it adds demand with almost no new labor [12:31].
- Use **companies** for specialization: huge throughput + cheaper private-queue construction = more output per worker and per Construction Point [15:02], [15:30].
- Pair specialization with trade to keep a flooded good's price (and profits) up; depend on trade more the smaller your market [16:00].
- New-player charter priority: focus on **monopolies** (keep prices high) and **industry charters** (flexibility); colonization charters are "useless but funny for roleplay" [17:02]–[17:30].
- Read the tooltips — much of the game's information lives there [19:00].
- Target end-state for the economy chapter: a specialized economy that employs all peasants and exports a company good, generating Investment Pool and income taxes to expand [20:00]–[20:31].
