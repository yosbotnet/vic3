# Belgium — Conquer, Colonize, Reaction (Comprehensive Tutorial 2025 synthesis)

**Source:** [The Comprehensive Victoria 3 Tutorial (2025) | Iberian Twilight](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P_PzglCFo9I) by Tarkusarkusar, 2025-12-16 — synthesis section [1:29:50] onward.

## What happened
After abandoning the demonstration Iberia save (which had lost half the country to a war fought purely to illustrate mechanics), the host relaunches as Belgium and, prompted by his co-hosts, sets three deliberately mixed goals: (1) conquer the Netherlands as historical-rival roleplay, (2) colonize the Congo, and (3) the "spanner" challenge — force Belgium, a structurally liberal country, into an absolutist conservative monarchy [1:30:00][1:30:30]. The run is an honest, semi-improvised teaching run with every failure narrated rather than cut. The economic opening goes cleanly: four construction sectors, iron mines plus railways to lift infrastructure, four decrees, a civilizing-mission tech path, "be friends with everyone except the target," companies given as much as possible, and a budget "differential diagnosis" that identifies iron as the most-consumed expensive input and works to lower its price. The two military goals fall almost trivially — Belgium becomes a **subject of Prussia** to ride its strong, ammunition-rich market (enabling skirmish infantry), wins the Netherlands war in months with Prussia called in, then deliberately **sabotages** the Prussian relationship to keep liberty desire above 25 and avoid being puppeted; the Congo is then conquered and left unincorporated (incorporating it would take 22 years due to wrong-culture homelands) to harvest low-wage, no-input plantation profits. The hard goal — authoritarianism — repeatedly backfires: a "big-brain" Belgian Plantation company meant to spawn supportive aristocrats instead spawns its HQ in the unincorporated Congo with local African aristocrats who have −50% pop political strength and can't be taxed, then relocates entirely when the colony rebels. Autocracy finally passes only by accident: a civil war / "reforms at gunpoint" event forces universal suffrage, which devalues votes so heavily that autocracy becomes trivial to pass, and the Congo rebellion conveniently removes its own radicals when the revolt is crushed. Belgium ends as an absolute monarchy — goals 1, 2 (partially), and 3 achieved — and the host closes by recommending Belgium as a learning start precisely because mistakes are punished visibly.

## Decisions made
- **Set deliberately mixed goals — conquer Netherlands, colonize Congo, force absolutism** [1:30:00][1:30:30]. Reasoning: roleplay the historical Belgian rivalry and Leopold's Congo, plus a co-host "spanner" to centralize power and make freedom "bad" — chosen because authoritarianism is hard and badly suited to liberal Belgium, so it stress-tests the lessons. The host notes everything still hinges on building the economy first.
- **Open with four construction sectors, then iron mines, then railways** [1:31:30][1:32:01]. Reasoning: spare budget is just dripping into the gold reserve and is better spent on buildings; construction needs iron, so queue iron mines alongside; iron mines cost infrastructure, so add railways to lift the cap (buildings take infrastructure, railways give it). The queue is reordered to bring infrastructure online faster.
- **Stack four decrees — road maintenance, resource-industry, agricultural, enlistment** [1:32:30][1:34:31]. Reasoning: authority is plentiful early and free to reclaim. Road maintenance gives construction efficiency + infrastructure where the build queue sits; resource-industry decree raises throughput in resource-heavy Wallonia (~26%); agricultural decree on farm-heavy Flanders (also to seed aristocrats/conservatives); enlistment to raise conscription for the Netherlands war.
- **Prioritize civilizing mission (tier 3) but queue all tier 2 techs first** [1:33:31][1:34:01]. Reasoning: military tech is already advanced enough, and civilizing mission is the key colonization tech — but you must clear tier 2 before a tier 3 unlocks, so queue the cheaper prerequisites rather than waiting on the target alone.
- **Be friends with everyone except the target** [1:34:01]. Reasoning: improving relations with France, Prussia, and Britain (the regional majors) for the cost of cheap influence keeps them from joining the Netherlands; later, accepting Britain's power-bloc invite is justified the same way — saying no would anger them [1:39:00].
- **Give companies as much as possible** [1:35:30][1:36:01]. Reasoning: the more of the economy a company controls, the more throughput it grants overall; hand the iron mines (and more) to John Cockerill and privatize them so the company auto-buys them.
- **Differential diagnosis on the budget — lower the price of the most-consumed expensive input (iron)** [1:38:00][1:41:00]. Reasoning: don't be fooled by big gold coins; the small per-unit number times huge consumption is what hurts. Iron is consumed ~4× more than the pricier-looking fabric, so iron is the real cost; expensive engines drive a £1,000 railway bill, so build motor industries too. House-style "differential diagnosis": change the cost of the input goods, not the spending.
- **Become a subject of Prussia to join its market, then win the Netherlands war fast** [1:44:31][1:45:31]. Reasoning: subjects share the overlord's market; Prussia's market is strong and ammunition-rich, which lets Belgium upgrade to skirmish infantry safely and call Prussia in as a free extra army. The host picks full subject over protectorate because it's a short-term run and he wants the war done — and cancels his own expensive conscripts once Prussia is backing him.
- **Sabotage the Prussian relationship to keep liberty desire above 25** [1:48:00]. Reasoning: a subject below 25 liberty desire can be turned into a puppet. Asking Prussia for something they'll refuse (e.g. market control) tanks relations on purpose, pushing liberty desire back up — a deliberate way to avoid annexation while keeping market access.
- **Leave the Congo unincorporated; incorporate only Holland** [1:46:31][1:55:00]. Reasoning: you can't tax unincorporated states, and incorporation costs bureaucracy — Holland takes ~27 months, but the Congo takes 22 years (a fifth of the game) because wrong-culture homelands share no heritage/language. So incorporate Holland for taxes, but keep the Congo unincorporated for collapsed wages and explosive plantation profits.
- **Use the violent-treatment ("chop hands") decree in the freshly conquered Congo** [1:56:30][1:57:01]. Reasoning: a just-conquered state has very high turmoil (radicalism), which makes building hugely inefficient. Violent treatment trades pop mortality for a reduced turmoil penalty, so construction runs less inefficiently while standard of living slowly recovers via market access.
- **Research malaria prevention (tier 4) to actually colonize the Congo** [2:01:30][2:02:01]. Reasoning: Congo states have malaria → −95% colony growth, so colonization barely progresses. Malaria prevention removes the effect everywhere; since it's tier 4 and slow, the host also builds government-only universities (no privatized universities exist) to push research, accepting the cost.
- **Build the Belgian Plantation company for colony growth, coffee prestige, and aristocrats** [2:07:30][2:08:00]. Reasoning: prosperity bonus is +10% colony growth (helps the Congo plan), its prestige good is coffee (which they're mass-producing), and its partial aristocratic ownership was meant to spawn aristocrats to support authoritarianism. This BACKFIRED — the HQ spawned in the unincorporated Congo with local African aristocrats at −50% pop political strength who can't be taxed, so the plan gave no political pull [2:14:30][2:15:01].
- **Apply the agricultural decree to the Congo plantations for pure-profit throughput** [2:12:31][2:13:00]. Reasoning: throughput normally scales inputs and outputs, but plantations have no inputs except wages, and throughput does not raise wages — so it's pure extra output, "pure money," like EU5 production efficiency.
- **Force authoritarianism by manipulating IG-leader ideology, reactionaries, and elections** [2:00:00][2:10:31]. Reasoning: ideology flows from characters and is "all gambling." ## Reusable lessons
- **Pick a goal before you touch the map.** Goals fall into GDP-max, standard-of-living-max, ideological (e.g. communism), historical roleplay, and challenge runs — but every one of them routes through the economy first. You can't pass Council Republic, hit 10M GDP, or colonize anything without building jobs and demand.
- **Conquer the weak, deal with the strong.** War is for things diplomacy can't buy; conquering weak neighbors costs almost nothing, while making deals (markets, friendships) with strong powers keeps you from being the weak one. Be friends with everyone except your actual target so nobody joins their side.
- **Queue tier-N+1 tech by filling its prerequisites, not by staring at the target.** A tier 3 tech like civilizing mission only unlocks after its tier 2 prerequisites, so queue the whole tier below it.
- **Diagnose the budget by consumption × price, not by sticker price.** Big gold coins lie; a modestly priced input consumed in huge volume (iron) costs far more than a flashy expensive one consumed rarely (fabric). Lower the price of the highest total-cost input by building more of it — a "differential diagnosis," not a spending cut.
- **Give companies as much of the economy as they'll take.** More buildings under a company means more throughput overall; privatize and let it auto-acquire.
- **Subjecthood is a market cheat with a leash — manage the leash.** Becoming a subject of a strong, resource-rich market (Prussia: ammunition → safe skirmish infantry) wins wars cheaply, but a subject below 25 liberty desire can be puppeted. Deliberately ask the overlord for things they'll refuse to keep liberty desire high.
- **Incorporate for taxes, stay unincorporated for profit.** You can't tax unincorporated land, and incorporation costs bureaucracy and time — trivial for same-culture states, but 22 years for wrong-culture homelands. Incorporate close-culture conquests; leave distant colonies unincorporated so collapsed wages flood plantation profits.
- **Plantation throughput is pure profit.** Plantations have no inputs except wages, and throughput doesn't raise wages — so any throughput bonus (agricultural decree, company) is straight output with no added cost. Pick plantation crops that match a prestige-goods company.
- **Violent-treatment decree trades lives for build efficiency in fresh conquests.** Just-conquered states have high turmoil that cripples construction; "chopping hands" reduces the turmoil penalty at the cost of pop mortality while SoL slowly recovers from market access.
- **Forcing reactionary politics on a liberal country is the hardest thing in the game, and it's mostly gambling.** Ideology comes from characters: exile-and-reload leader rolls, bolster reactionaries to raise authoritarian odds, install authoritarian IG leaders to flip a party, and rig elections — but each costs legitimacy/electoral confidence and frequently backfires (a general makes his own party; a king turns arrogant and kills your legitimacy mid-vote).
- **Watch where company HQs and aristocrats actually spawn.** A company built to seed authoritarian aristocrats is useless if its HQ lands in an unincorporated state — those pops have −50% political strength and can't be taxed. Mechanics can quietly invert your plan.
- **Crises can resolve your goals by accident — let some of them run.** A "reforms at gunpoint" civil war forced universal suffrage, which devalued votes enough to make autocracy trivial; suppressing a colonial rebellion conveniently killed off its radicals. Sometimes the cheapest path to a law is the disaster you didn't plan.
- **Belgium is the best learning start because failure is loud.** On an easy country you can play wrong and never notice; as Belgium a mistake gets you conquered or stalls your economy visibly, so you actually learn. The host's real teaching method is "just play and learn as you go."
