# War — Comprehensive Tutorial 2025 (Tarkusarkusar)
**Source:** [The Comprehensive Victoria 3 Tutorial (2025) | Iberian Twilight](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P_PzglCFo9I) by Tarkusarkusar, 2025-12-16 — chapter "5. War" [59:30].

## Summary
War is diplomacy's most important interwoven mechanic. You enter war through a **Diplomatic Play** — which Tarkus insists is just "a war with a 100-day waiting period." Plays generate **infamy**, drag third parties in, and let both sides set war goals in a game of chicken. The army is composed of regiments with offense/defense/occupation stats; you mobilize them, assign them to fronts, and set generals to attack or defend. You win by grinding the enemy's **war support** to zero (capitulation) by occupying their war-goal states and capital. The chapter's central thesis: **war is a macro game, not a micro game** — it's about whether your economy can afford the military goods, and about the opportunity cost of those goods and conscripted pops versus construction and factory work. Practical bottom line: fight weak, low-infamy targets (Africa, Asia, Middle East), avoid fighting Europeans and great powers, and never be afraid to reload — and never play Iron Man because the game is too random. (Tarkus loses the demonstration war against the Netherlands/Britain on camera.)

## Core mechanics  (numbered; each key claim with a real [mm:ss] or [h:mm:ss])
1. **You can't declare war during a truce** — Tarkus has a truce with the Netherlands and must wait it out before attacking [59:34].
2. **The war-declaration list predicts who joins which side**, e.g. Sweden predicted to join the Netherlands, Britain ambivalent (could join either side), Prussia more likely to join your side. This screen did not exist in original Victoria 3; it was added in a later update — before it you declared blind [1:00:01].
3. **The prediction is not very useful** because it doesn't account for the infamy from the play itself shifting relations once declared [1:00:30].
4. **A "diplomatic incident" is just the game's word for a war.** "Diplomatic plays are wars but with a 100-day waiting period. That's all it is." [1:00:30].
5. **Sway / obligations:** during the play you can use the **Sway** button to bring a third party in. Prussia would help if you became their protectorate, or in exchange for an **obligation** — an IOU that lets them later increase the accept-chance on a treaty (with "repudiated obligation" penalties if you refuse). Tarkus offers Prussia an obligation to join [1:01:00]–[1:02:31].
6. **Bringing in an ally can drag in their enemies** — if the ally you sway has bad relations with another power, that power may get called in against you, just like the defender can call in allies [1:02:00].
7. **War goals:** every play has at least one **primary war goal** (the one it started with); you can add **secondary goals** that are only enforced if you actually win the war [1:02:31]–[1:03:01].
8. **Backing down during the 100-day buildup** grants the *other* side their **primary** war goal only, not their secondaries. It's intended as a game of chicken; the AI weighs the ramifications of losing big secondary goals when deciding whether to back down [1:03:01].
9. **"Make primary" button** (added in a recent patch): converts a secondary goal into a primary one so it's *also* enforced if the enemy backs down [1:08:30]–[1:09:01].
10. **Infamy refund is only partial** when adding war goals and the enemy backs down [1:03:31].
11. **Infamy thresholds:** at **25** nations distrust you (diplomacy harder); at **50** most nations will join plays against you if you get aggressive; at **100** great powers actively work to rip you to shreds [1:03:31]–[1:04:00].
12. **High infamy also makes your subjects disloyal** (seeking other great powers' support) and makes other nations **embargo** you, harming your economy. Keep infamy low unless you desperately need the war goals [1:04:00].
13. **Infamy is contextual:** attacking unrecognized nations is much cheaper; taking low-population land, or land with a population similar to your primary culture, costs much less infamy [1:04:31].
14. **Army = regiments, each with stats.** Line infantry has ~20 offense (+ modifier) and ~25–26 defense [1:05:00].
15. **Unit-role rule of thumb:** infantry are good at defending, artillery good at attacking, cavalry good at occupying [1:05:30].
16. **Early-game artillery is bad; cavalry is just better.** A cannon (25 atk / 15 def) is outclassed by a Lancer (31 off / 21 def) which has more stats in every way, plus more battle occupation and kill rate — the cannon only has more devastation (more area damage) [1:05:30]–[1:06:01].
17. **Mobilization** makes the army buy more goods (costs rise) — Tarkus watches upkeep go from ~2.64k to ~3.11k on mobilizing, with prices fluctuating from trade [1:06:31]–[1:07:03].
18. **Mobilization options** are bonuses you grant units (extra supplies, and later flamethrowers, chemical weapons / mustard gas, machine gunners) — each raises input/upkeep costs in goods in exchange for army stats; you unlock more as you research tech [1:07:30], [1:09:31]–[1:10:00].
19. **Costs are paid in goods, not money** — you pay for the goods the army consumes [1:07:30].
20. **Fronts:** assign an army to a front via the HQ ("deploy the front"). Generals have hidden locations; you decide whether each attacks or defends. Army offense vs defense stats shown (e.g. 24.5 offense / 25.7 defense → better at defending) [1:10:00]–[1:11:01].
21. **The front color gauge** (Hearts of Iron-like) tells you roughly how likely you are to win: green = probably winning, red = probably losing. If red, set all generals to defend [1:11:01].
22. **General orders / tactics** (e.g. "adamant defense": consumes more goods, gives defense + better tactic chance) exist but there are only ~4 in the game; tactics are pure gamble you can't influence [1:11:30].
23. **Why conquer:** you gain access to population, resources, and buildings — conquered businesses keep running and pay money to you (its capitalists). The greatest reason of all is depriving rivals of those things [1:11:30], [1:13:32].
24. **Building ownership is not always local.** Conquering a state where financial districts own buildings elsewhere transfers that ownership to your capitalists; e.g. conquering Britain's home counties hands you financial districts owning businesses "as far as Canada" [1:12:01]–[1:13:32].
25. **Two ways to raise troops:** professional/regular army (red icons) or conscripts (gray icons). A professional regiment takes ~9 weeks to build [1:14:01].
26. **One regiment = 1,000 men** (Tarkus first misremembers as 3,000, then corrects) [1:14:32].
27. **Conscripts are built instantly** but then "raise conscripts" slowly pulls pops into the conscript building; they arrive as low-experience recruits (worse fighters) but you can get a lot of them quickly. You still pay for their equipment, so cost rises as conscript count grows [1:14:32]–[1:15:30].
28. **Conscripts' real purpose is to inflate army size past what your economy can normally afford** — your professional army size is an accurate measure of the army you can actually sustain. Conscripts are unsustainable long-term but war is short-term [1:16:00].
29. **War support and capitulation:** war support (e.g. Belgium 73.5) drops from radical population, hostile lobby clout (a pro-British lobby hurts support when fighting Britain), and especially **taking casualties**. The Netherlands lose more war support because they're being occupied [1:16:30]–[1:17:31].
30. **Capitulation desire** is an AI-only number (mostly derived from war support); when it hits zero the AI always leaves the war — even an ally can bail while you're winning, costing you the war (Prussia leaves and Tarkus loses) [1:17:31]–[1:18:30].
31. **War support can't drop unless all the enemy's war goals (or their capital) are occupied.** If you can't occupy what you're demanding, the enemy will never surrender → Victoria 3's **forever-war problem** (Tarkus shows the enemy at "negative a thousand reasons to accept") [1:18:30]–[1:19:30].
32. **Wars are mostly all-or-nothing** — the AI doesn't like mixing/matching war goals, so you generally fully win or fully lose [1:18:02].
33. **War is a macro game, not micro.** Plan around your economy: can you afford the goods to click the army bonuses? If you grant goods you don't have (sugar, tobacco, liquor, opium not in your market), you get **shortages**, which **cap organization** — a 36% shortage caps org at 63%, crippling the army [1:20:31]–[1:21:31].
34. **Never grant goods you don't have.** In older patches granting absent opium was still net-positive; now organization penalties make it "truly killer" [1:21:02]–[1:21:31].
35. **Don't fight Europeans — fight the periphery.** Zulu have 0 battalions + max 3 conscripts (3 units) vs Britain's 120 + 54 conscripts; the Netherlands have 13. The weak are obviously easier and still hold value (Zulu: 55 coal mines, 300k pops; Sokoto: high-population area) — the catch is you'll discriminate those pops [1:22:00]–[1:23:00].

## Common pitfalls
- **Trusting the join-prediction screen** — it ignores the infamy/relation hit your own play causes, so the forecast can be wrong (Britain ended up joining against Tarkus) [1:00:30], [1:07:03].
- **Adding war goals carelessly** — infamy is only partially refunded if the enemy backs down [1:03:31].
- **Letting infamy climb** — at 50 everyone piles on against you; at 100 great powers and disloyal subjects can destroy you, plus embargoes [1:04:00].
- **Fighting Europeans / great powers** — high infamy and high cost of war for value you could get cheaper in Africa/Asia [1:04:31], [1:21:31].
- **Relying on a swayed ally** — if their capitulation desire hits zero they leave mid-war and you lose, even while winning (Prussia) [1:17:31]–[1:18:30].
- **Declaring without the means to occupy your war goals** — you can get stuck in an unwinnable, unloseable forever war [1:18:30]–[1:19:30].
- **Granting army bonuses for goods you don't produce** — shortages cap organization and make the army useless [1:21:02]–[1:21:31].
- **Playing Iron Man** — the game is too random/unpredictable; reload instead [1:20:00].

## Reusable lessons / decision rules
- **Treat every Diplomatic Play as a real war with a 100-day timer** — it generates infamy and drags in third parties regardless of the "diplomatic incident" label [1:00:30].
- **Only start plays against targets you can easily defeat and that cost little infamy** — Africa, Asia, the Middle East, at most weak European powers [1:04:31].
- **Keep infamy low** unless you desperately need specific war goals; respect the 25/50/100 thresholds [1:04:00].
- **Early game: prefer cavalry over artillery** (cavalry is strictly better until later tech) [1:06:01].
- **Set defensive when the front gauge is red**; pick a tactic order like adamant defense, but accept battle tactics are gambling [1:11:01]–[1:11:30].
- **Choose professional vs conscript on economics, not combat** — professionals are quick, reinforced, and always consuming goods; conscripts inflate size beyond what you can afford, for short wars only [1:16:00]–[1:16:30].
- **You can only force surrender by occupying the enemy's war goals (or capital)** — don't demand what you can't take, or you'll get a forever war [1:18:30]–[1:19:30].
- **Plan war economically: never grant goods you don't have** (shortage → org cap) [1:21:02].
- **War is opportunity cost:** military goods could have built construction; conscripted pops could have made high-profit goods in factories. Use war only for what diplomacy can't get — conquer the weak (cost ≈ nothing), deal with the strong [1:23:00]–[1:23:31].
- **Don't be afraid to reload; avoid Iron Man** — the game is too random [1:20:00].
