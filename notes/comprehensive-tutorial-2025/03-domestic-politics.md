# Domestic Politics — Comprehensive Tutorial 2025 (Tarkusarkusar)
**Source:** [The Comprehensive Victoria 3 Tutorial (2025) | Iberian Twilight](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P_PzglCFo9I) by Tarkusarkusar, 2025-12-16 — chapter "3. Domestic Politics" [36:11].

## Summary
Pops generate political power based on their wealth, and they put that power behind an Interest Group, where it is called clout. Clout determines how much an IG can help or hinder law changes — itself a "gambling system" of success/stall chances rolled each cycle. Outside the government, Political Movements pursue their own ideologies and can escalate to civil war. Legitimacy, Institutions/bureaucracy, and (DLC) agitators round out the chapter. The presenter frames all of this as "not very complicated" and delivers it fully scripted with no stream clips.

## Core mechanics  (numbered; each key claim with a real [mm:ss])
1. **Political power comes from wealth; voting laws flatten it.** A pop's *job* determines political *alignment*; a pop's *wealth* determines political *power* — rich people have more influence than poor [36:00]. The wealth-disparity in power can be reduced with voting laws: the more suffrage you grant, the more each individual vote counts relative to wealth [36:00]. Even at universal suffrage, wealth still affects political power (abstracted as the rich lobbying politicians / buying ads) [36:31].
2. **Clout = pop political power placed behind an IG.** Pops put their political power behind an Interest Group; that power is called clout. An IG's clout directly translates to how much power it has in government, which translates to how much it will help or hinder law changes you attempt [36:31].
3. **IG ideologies: inherent (red) vs leader (blue).** Each IG has a set of ideologies defining which laws they want. Red ideologies are inherent to the IG and don't change; blue is the *leader* ideology, which can supersede the red ones [37:01]. Example: the Church has the *moralist* ideology (endorses state religion, opposes state atheism) and is *pious* (wants religious schools) [37:01].
4. **Leader ideology can override the IG's baseline.** The leader is the person in charge of the IG and brings an extra ideology. Example: Church leader "Nimi" is a *reformer* — he wants cultural exclusion, primary schools, and limited women's rights [37:01]. The women's-rights stance conflicts with the Church's baseline *patriarchal* ideology, but because the current leader is a reformer, his preference supersedes the red one, so the Church supports expanding women's rights while he leads [37:31].
5. **Leader succession is randomized ("gambling mechanic").** When a leader dies, they're replaced by a random new leader. The chance of each leader ideology is viewable on mouseover; in the example there's a good chance of a *theocrat* due to pressure from the Catholic traditionalist movement [38:01].
6. **IG approval gives buffs/debuffs.** Approval is a third key IG stat. Low approval applies a debuff — for the Church, an education-access debuff, which lowers literacy nationwide (education access is how pops gain literacy) [38:01]. At 5 approval the Church gives extra authority; at 10 approval it gives extra birth rate. Each IG has different buffs/debuffs and ideologies [38:30].
7. **The three IG stats to watch:** clout, approval, and leader ideology [38:30].
8. **Law changes are driven by IG clout (top-down) as a chance-based process.** IGs represent top-down government — they run the country and can use their clout to pass laws. Each clout percentage directly translates to your chance of changing laws (another gambling system) [38:30]. Example: passing *Laissez-Faire* is supported by Industrialists (inherent laissez-faire ideology) and Petite Bourgeoisie (their leader is a *market liberal*), but opposed by Landowners and Farmers [39:02].
9. **Law-passing has named steps that are cosmetic.** Burgundy's steps are introduction → consideration → adoption; America's *tenant farmers* law uses sponsorship → study → voting. The names differ by your distribution of power but are "just names" — there's no functional difference between them [39:02].
10. **Success vs stall on each cycle.** Each rotation of the blue ring carries a *success chance* (likelihood you advance to the next step) and a *stall chance* (chance of an event that hinders passing the law) [39:30]. Mousing over the law-passing box shows *debate* and *advance* chances: debates can be good, bad, or demand a sacrifice for success chance; advancements don't push to the next phase but grant a success bonus or a national benefit [40:00]. You wait for the blue ring to fill, then an event happens or you get a success [40:00].
11. **Negotiation button (recent update — Iberian Twilight free patch).** Added "just a few days ago" with the Iberian Twilight accompanying free patch. Clicking it opens a list of IGs that could be negotiated with to put their clout behind a law they'd otherwise oppose [40:00]. In the laissez-faire example, only the Armed Forces were willing to negotiate [40:00].
12. **Political Movements = pops acting outside government.** The big circle with revolutionary vibes and a filling red ring is an *insurrectionary political movement* — the political aspirations of pops working outside the government. They're listed in the government tab and, like IGs, have ideologies they back [40:31]. Pops support movements independently of voting, so even a fully authoritarian state can have movements rise up [41:00].
13. **Movement escalation → civil war.** A movement's *support* number is akin to clout; its *activism* is akin to approval [41:00]. At 60% activism a movement becomes insurrectionary (threatening civil war) and a bad event fires every time the red ring fills. At 100% activism it starts a civil war — and if you lose the civil war, it's game over [41:00].
14. **Movements affect law passing.** Primary source of activism: supporting pops being radicals and you passing laws they dislike [41:00]. Above 25% activism, a movement uses its support number to affect your chance to pass laws [41:31].
15. **Legitimacy comes from your distribution-of-power laws.** Legitimacy = how respected/legitimate your government is, determined by distribution-of-power laws — do you have elections, a king whose IG is your main legitimacy source, how high taxes are, among other factors [42:00]. Burgundy (autocracy) derives most legitimacy from the government matching the king's ideology; the king is Petite Bourgeoisie, so a government run by them alone maximizes legitimacy [42:00].
16. **Legitimacy effects.** High legitimacy → faster law changes and (chiefly) more loyalist generation. Low legitimacy → loss of IG approval, slower law changes, and more radicals [42:31].
17. **Institutions cost bureaucracy.** Certain laws create institutions (e.g. education). In the institution tab you spend bureaucracy to increase an institution's size for buffs, but you must maintain that bureaucracy [43:00]. Bureaucracy comes from the government administration building (always government-run; can get very expensive) [43:00]. Most institutions are worthwhile, but watch how expensive bureaucracy gets [43:00].
18. **Agitators (DLC feature).** You can invite people to your country to start or support political movements — e.g. inviting Louis Napoleon Bonaparte gives support to the reactionary movement. Once present, an agitator can be made a general or given leadership of an IG [43:30].

## Numbers & formulas  (with [mm:ss]; omit if none)
- **IG approval thresholds:** Church gives extra authority at **5** approval and extra birth rate at **10** approval [38:01].
- **Negotiation amendability threshold:** the amendability number must be **above 25** for an IG to negotiate at all; the lower the number, the more they demand when you do negotiate [40:31].
- **Movement insurrection threshold:** **60%** activism = insurrectionary (civil war threat); **100%** activism = civil war begins [41:00].
- **Movement law-influence threshold:** above **25%** activism, a movement uses its support to affect law-passing chances [41:31].
- **Worked example:** the laissez-faire stall chance had an extra **+5%** because the labor movement had **94%** activism and **5%** support [41:31].
- **Clout source:** mouse over the clout button to see derivation; in the example the Industrialists derive their political power from wealth, and their supporters are mostly capitalists and engineers [44:01].

## Common pitfalls
- **Government churn.** Don't reform/change governments repeatedly — every change gains radicals and loses loyalists [42:00].
- **Over-analyzing legitimacy.** Don't think too much about legitimacy; just click "reform government" and pick from the list the game offers — those are the three most legitimate governments available at the time [42:31].
- **Runaway bureaucracy cost.** Bureaucracy (government administration building) can get very expensive; keep the price of **paper** low to keep costs down [43:30].
- **Ignoring activism.** A movement above 25% activism actively worsens your law-passing chances, and 60%+ risks civil war / game-over — don't let radical pops accumulate unchecked [41:00].

## Reusable lessons / decision rules
- **Watch three IG stats: clout, approval, leader ideology** — they tell you who can pass your laws and what they'll back [38:30].
- **Read tooltips.** Mouse over clout to see how an IG's power is calculated; mouse over leader-ideology chances and the law box (debate/advance) to understand the rolls [44:01]. "Sometimes you don't need to know how something works, you just need to know how to work with it" [44:30].
- **Use the negotiation button (new) to buy clout for stalled laws** — only IGs with amendability > 25 are reachable, and lower amendability costs more [40:31].
- **Keep activism / radicals down to protect both law-passing and your survival** (60% insurrection, 100% civil war = game over) [41:00].
- **For legitimacy, just pick from "reform government";** avoid repeated government swaps that breed radicals [42:31].
- **Pay for institutions deliberately:** institutions are mostly worth it, but budget for the bureaucracy and keep paper cheap [43:00].
