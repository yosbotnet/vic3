# Case Study: The Great Wave (Japan)

## Overview

This is a 17-episode synthesis of Flying Dutchman's 1836-start Japan campaign on the Great Wave DLC (patches 1.13.0 through 1.13.3). The run opens as the Tokugawa Shogunate under Sakoku and Closed Borders, holding ~16.6M starving pops, locked behind the Edo Status caste system, sitting on a 12-year Tempo Crisis timer, and unable to enact most laws because the cabinet is dominated by a single hostile interest group (the Daimyo). The campaign's arc is the modernization of Japan as a piece of political engineering — not a war story. The end state is the Empire of Japan: a recognized Great Power, formed power bloc ("Empire of the Rising Sun"), serfdom abolished, constitutional monarchy with parties, Per Capita Taxation, Appointed Bureaucrats, Responsible Government, three SE Asian protectorates, and ~5th GDP in the world.

The run is honest about its failures. The Tempo Crisis was conceded as unwinnable [ep06]; an election meddle backfired into an illegitimate government [ep12]; a Taiwan invasion had to be save-scummed back [ep14]; a fresh Bornean protectorate flipped back to independence because the player was busy elsewhere [ep17]; and the final episode ended early with an unexplained 6M unemployed-laborer anomaly [ep17]. The case study foregrounds those misfires alongside the wins, because most of the campaign's reusable lessons come from the decisions made when things went sideways.

## Campaign arc

### Phase 1 — Opening the closed country (ep01–ep03)

Japan starts crippled. Sakoku closes the market, Closed Borders blocks migration, the famine event "Era of Heavenly Protection" fires turn one [ep01], and the Daimyo's dominance means almost no law has a non-trivial pass chance. The opening 12-year window is repositioning, not legislating: rather than burn enactment attempts on doomed laws, the player spends authority on Promote Social Mobility decrees in every state to fight the Edo Status qualifications penalty [ep01], targets the *reformer* side of the Tempo Crisis (so the completion bonus buffs the Chonin, the only IG that wants the laws Japan needs) [ep01, ep04], and trims the army down to exactly the 50-battalion / 500 Power Projection threshold to free upkeep [ep02].

Construction is cheapened before anything ambitious: logging camps in Kyushu/Shikoku, then a tooling workshop co-located with an iron mine in Kanto (avoiding the state-vs-market price gap that wrecks isolated economies), then the sawmill PM, then iron-tools — construction cost falls from ~32k to ~22k inside one episode [ep02]. Shipyards are sliced 100% to supply ships because the starting Merchant Navy law gives +30% supply-ship efficiency, and military hulls require artillery/iron Japan does not yet make [ep01]. The Devout are bribed with cheap political concessions to lift Dedicated Police Force from 0% to ~14%; it passes on the second attempt with a deliberate setback [ep02].

The phase ends when the Miroslavski incident fires. The player takes the "review Sakoku" branch (the only one that unlocks Revoke Sakoku), revokes immediately, swaps to Mercantilism, and burns the "Picking the Lock" modifier (Chonin/Rangakusha/industrialists +25% strength for 10 years) as early as possible to overlap the entire window with the upcoming law-change queue [ep03]. Ryukyu is annexed via Military Access Treaty + Money Transfer + Obligation — a stack of cheap diplomatic pacts that outscored Qing's naval-projection-driven sway lead without building expensive infrastructure in the contested state [ep03].

### Phase 2 — The Honorable Restoration scramble (ep04–ep08)

The middle phase is a race against two overlapping timers: the Tempo Crisis (still running) and the new Honorable Restoration agenda. The player chases Tempo's reformer goals by pushing Chonin clout above 10%, building Naval Fortifications in Kanto and Osaka to trigger the Edo-Osaka Confiscation Order (10-Re Consolidation Act: -decree cost + +30% tax in the two richest agriculture states for 20 years), and enacting Capital Fleets [ep04, ep05]. Decree-cost reducers stack: Imperious ruler trait + Consolidation Act drops state decree cost to ~40, at which point agricultural decrees become efficient throughput buffs [ep04].

A naval doctrine is set: only build Ships of the Line, upgraded to iron-reinforced hull (55 armor) — frigates can't damage SOLs at this tier, and 55 armor shuts down enemy medium guns [ep06]. Naval Administration is built as a parallel chain because crewing is a *separate* problem from building, and shipyards have no input-shortage penalty so hulls can be mass-produced before the artillery economy catches up [ep05, ep06].

The Tempo Crisis is formally conceded at 6/8 [ep06]: Chonin clout collapsed to ~7 with no path back inside the window. Sunk-cost discipline beats the temptation to keep grinding; the freed law slot goes straight into Tenant Farmers, ending serfdom after a deliberately bolstered Peasant Movement [ep06, ep07]. A samurai coup is defeated with the +15 coup-resistance church decision (not the +coup-strength alternative) [ep06]. Emperor Ninko dies and is replaced by the 15-year-old Komei.

Imperial Restoration is then engineered manually. The player deliberately enacts laws the Sonno Joi movement *opposes* (rigid Edo status, Trade Privileges treaties to minors) to spike its activism over 50% [ep07]. The Restoration button is save-scummed: the *high-legitimacy* attempt produces Taisei Hokan (emperor reaffirms shogun — bad); a reload, then mass-anger interest groups to tank legitimacy, then click — produces the Decree of Restoration that abolishes the shogunate [ep07]. The Tokugawa fall, Japan becomes the Empire of Japan, and the indefinite Bakumatsu modifier (+500% political activism) begins.

The Komei Restoration completes one month later [ep08]: capital moves to Kanto, Daimyo convert into the Kazoku, and three 12-year journal entries open simultaneously — Dispatch a Mission to the West, Industrialize Japan, Retire the Samurai. Maeda Yoshiyasu (Chonin leader, sick but ambitious) is sent on the Iwakura-equivalent tour: short stops in Mexico, long stays in Britain and France; the mission report picks Public Schools (institution) over a one-off research speed bonus [ep08]. A second coup is defused without combat by simply un-assigning the rebelling general — a general with no command produces zero coup progress [ep08].

### Phase 3 — Recognition and the constitutional pivot (ep09–ep12)

Recognition is the bottleneck for everything: loan interest is 26% as unrecognized, which makes any borrowing ruinous and turns the campaign into a treasury triage exercise [ep07, ep09]. The player cancels trade-privilege treaties, refuses to subsidize ports, sells government-owned buildings to private, raises consumption taxes, lowers wages, and pauses construction in that order [ep07, ep09]. Foreign investment rights are granted to Austria as a cheap diplomatic currency to unlock the alliance / treaty path needed for recognition (Kazoku/samurai approval is the price) [ep08, ep11].

The path to Industrialize Japan runs through laws that need IGs the cabinet does not yet contain. Lex Edo Status, the prerequisite for replacing Warrior Caste, is unpassable directly. The intermediate move is Landed Voting: the landowners want it, passing it converts Japan to constitutional monarchy with parties, and the new cabinet can be stacked with Chonin / Intelligentsia / Gosho / Trade Unions [ep10]. Political concessions to landowners on Lex Edo Status are accepted as effectively *free* because the concession dissolves the moment the law passes — pick concessions whose value collapses with the law you're enacting [ep10]. Stalled enactments are then learned to be tick-deterministic: reloading and re-clicking the same day produces the same stall result — the only lever is to change the stall-chance modifier *before* the next monthly tick (negotiate, bribe an IG leader, accept costly demands), then advance time [ep11].

Recognition lands Dec 1859 via the Treaty of Commerce and Navigation with Austria, dropping loan interest from 26% to ~7.5% [ep11]. The Power Bloc "Empire of the Rising Sun" is formed under the Sovereign Empire (Vassalization) principle — formed *with no vassals*, because the surplus diplomatic influence pool already exceeds what's needed, and the formation tooltip no longer requires vassals [ep09, ep11]. Industrialize Japan completes with 1y6m to spare by spamming Government Administrations (250 construction, +20 urbanization per level, downgradable) across qualifying states to satisfy the "urban center L5 in 70% of states" gate — the urbanization speedrun building [ep11, ep12].

The phase's defining failure is an election meddle for the Constitutional Reform Party that flips an IG into opposition, leaves Japan with an illegitimate government, blocks Total Separation of Church and State mid-enactment, and forces the player to *cancel* the law to restore legitimacy [ep12]. Recovery move: enact Appointed Bureaucrats instead, because the post-coalition composition supports it and it directly fixes the chronic taxation-capacity shortfall [ep12].

### Phase 4 — Economy under stress, Korea aborted (ep13–ep15)

Cost-of-living dominates the late game. Groceries are eating the lowest strata's budget; paper and iron are the two largest *government* line items (paper ~38k, iron ~46k of state spending) [ep13]. The lesson: audit government goods, not just market profitability — for goods the state itself is a net buyer of, building dedicated supply specifically to *crush the price* is worth more than the building's own balance sheet. A "Japanese become obsessed with paper" event is declined for exactly this reason [ep13]. Fertilizer plants are queued (5 plants × 3 sulfur mines × iron) to stabilize food; one grocery industry per state — not one mega-factory — to suppress local prices, because consumer goods scale via state-local supply [ep13, ep14].

The Korea invasion is aborted twice. First, Taiwan: declared because "Qing has no big navy," then save-scummed back when the target province turns out to be tagged South China with 270 garrisoned defenders, and the player's own navy organization collapses for unexplained supply-ship reasons [ep14]. The lesson: state-region ownership and garrison count must be checked *before* declaring, and "weak navy" does not imply "weak coastal defense." Naval construction pivots away from SOLs toward frigates and cruisers because the binding constraint is *transport capacity per construction-week* for an upcoming sealift, not hull strength [ep13]. Ship-design points spent on speed above the fleet's slowest hull are wasted, so frigate templates drop speed to minimum and reinvest into screening/hull/carrying [ep13].

Per Capita Taxation enacts after *four* setbacks — the law has a peasant-share crossover point (~70%) where it becomes a net gain because the employed-pop tax base is now large enough; persisting through enactment failures was correct once the crossover was reached [ep13, ep15]. A dedicated 20-unit landing-marine corps is built because every other infantry type carries -25% naval-invasion efficiency; marines pay ~50% more goods upkeep for identical combat stats and zero invasion penalty [ep14, ep15]. The Emperor in Parliament journal entry is resolved into Responsible Government, replacing absolutists with royalists [ep15] — cleaner IG landscape, no civil war. Emperor Komei dies at 37; the Meiji era begins with a modernizer ruler granting +25% prominence to the Gosho [ep15].

### Phase 5 — Power-bloc expansion and unraveling (ep16–ep17)

With Appointed Bureaucrats finally passing after multiple election resets [ep16], Japan turns outward. Power-projection level 2 (reduced infamy) enables a protectorate-first expansion strategy against weak SE Asian neighbors: Brunei backs down without fighting [ep16], Sulu is defeated and made a protectorate via marine naval invasion despite losing one earlier battle [ep16], Lanfang vassalizes peacefully [ep17]. Protectorates yield power-bloc income (+500/Brunei noted), a power-bloc member, and a path to later annexation at lower infamy than direct conquest.

The wheels come off in ep17. Bulongan is conquered and made a protectorate, then immediately broken free under the "failed to protect" mechanic because the player was tied up in another diplomatic play during the truce window [ep17]. A liberation attempt on Joseon (Qing subject) is aborted because liberty desire never climbs — "support independence" only works if LD is already trending. Bureaucracy oscillates despite repeated GA builds; paper costs consume ~60k of 160k income. Companies are unlocked but the obvious Japanese flavour picks (Mitsubishi for paper/iron, Mitsui for clothing/dyes/silk) require prerequisites not yet built. The episode ends early after the player discovers ~6 million unemployed laborers with no buildings able to absorb them — suspected post-patch save state corruption rather than a strategic failure, but the underlying lesson stands: building construction must keep pace with pop growth, or radicals self-sustain past any law/decree intervention.

## Key strategic decisions

- **Target the reformer side of the Tempo Crisis, not the hardliner side** [ep01, ep04]. The choice is about which IG gets the +20% pop-attraction completion bonus, not flavor. Reformer feeds the Chonin, who are the only IG that wants the bureaucracy and Lex Status laws needed to open Japan. The alternative (hardliner) would have buffed the Samurai and locked the country into the laws Japan was trying to escape. The campaign later conceded Tempo as unwinnable, but the *direction* of effort was still correct because the same Chonin work fed the Honorable Restoration.

- **Fire the "Picking the Lock" modifier as early as possible by revoking Sakoku immediately** [ep03]. The alternative was to delay revocation to grow the National Guard institution and Chonin clout first. The reasoning: Picking the Lock is a one-shot 10-year +25% strength modifier for Chonin/Rangakusha/industrialists — earlier activation maximizes the overlap with the upcoming law-change queue. Time-limited "opening" modifiers are launch windows, not buffs to hoard.

- **Annex Ryukyu via stacked diplomatic pacts, not infrastructure** [ep03]. Alternatives: build a level-2 port or shipyard in Ryukyu (huge infra commitment), or the state-religion-gated Kiku Ogimi path (law-locked). Chose Military Access Treaty + Money Transfer (maxed cash to maximize obligation points) + Mutual Obligation — three cheap pacts that together yield +monthly sway and beat Qing's naval-projection-driven contest score. Generalizes to any sway contest: find the cheapest interaction unlock, stack multiple low-cost pacts, max the cash on transfer deals.

- **Concede the Tempo Crisis at 6/8 to free law focus for serfdom abolition** [ep06]. Math was unrecoverable: Chonin clout had collapsed to ~7 with no path back inside the timer. Sunk-cost discipline beat the temptation to keep grinding. The freed law slot went straight into Tenant Farmers, ending serfdom — a higher-leverage outcome than completing Tempo would have been.

- **Engineer the Imperial Restoration by deliberately tanking shogun legitimacy before clicking** [ep07]. The same button produces *different branches* at different legitimacy levels: high legitimacy → Taisei Hokan (emperor reaffirms shogun, bad outcome); low legitimacy → Decree of Restoration (abolishes shogunate). Save before, click to test the branch, reload, mass-anger IGs to tank legitimacy, click again. The "unforeseen consequences" warning at low legitimacy is the *desired* path for the restoration arc — read it as a signpost, not a deterrent. Spiked Sonno Joi above 50% in the same window by enacting laws the movement opposed (rigid Edo status, Trade Privileges to minors).

- **Enact Landed Voting as an intermediate step to unblock Lex Edo Status** [ep10]. Direct attempts at Lex Edo Status under the Kazoku-dominated cabinet were "almost impossible." Landed Voting is wanted by landowners — passing it converts Japan to constitutional monarchy with parties, after which the cabinet can be stacked with Chonin/Intelligentsia/Gosho/Trade Unions to actually pass the law that was the original target. Generalizes to: when a critical law is unpassable, route through a cabinet-reshaping law first, even if the intermediate law is not what you wanted.

- **Form the Power Bloc "Empire of the Rising Sun" with zero vassals under Sovereign Empire** [ep09, ep11]. Discovered the formation tooltip no longer requires vassals once influence is high enough; the bonuses on bloc leadership are immediate. Deferred activation until *after* recognition because forming early would have tanked the -60 relations with other GPs that were needed for the recognition grind. The Vassalization principle then paired with Great Power status and powered the later SE Asian protectorate expansion.

- **Spam Government Administrations as the urbanization speedrun building** [ep11, ep12]. Industrialize Japan requires urban center L5 in 70% of states inside 12 years. Organic industrial growth would have missed the window. GAs cost ~250 construction (vs 400 for University/Art Academy), give +20 urbanization per level, build extremely fast, and are *downgradable* — spam to meet the threshold, then sell down once the journal completes (paper upkeep bleeds the treasury if you keep them). Cheapest urbanization-per-construction-point beats per-level comparison.

- **Build dedicated landing marines instead of reusing line troops for naval invasions** [ep14, ep15]. Every other infantry type (skirmish, mobile, artillery, shrapnel) carries -25% naval invasion efficiency; marines pay ~50% more goods upkeep for identical combat stats and zero invasion penalty. Pre-built the ammunition + small arms supply chain before retraining, because consumable-dependent unit unlocks collapse on first engagement if the input factories aren't online.

- **Pivot the late-game economy around government-good prices, not building profitability** [ep13]. State spending revealed paper (~38k) and iron (~46k) were the two largest line items. Building paper mills and iron mines specifically to *crush their market price* saved more than the buildings' own profit. Declined a "Japanese become obsessed with paper" event for exactly this reason — pop-demand modifiers on goods the state is a net buyer of are a tax on the treasury.

- **Switch from direct conquest to protectorate-first expansion** [ep16, ep17]. Brunei, Sulu, Lanfang were Made Subjects rather than conquered: less infamy, immediate power-bloc income (+500/Brunei), and the option to demote-to-puppet or annex later when liberty desire drops. The campaign's one ignored caveat — that "failed to protect" auto-independence will wipe a fresh subject if the player is mid-play elsewhere — cost Bulongan [ep17].

## Reusable lessons

- **When the starting cabinet is locked by one dominant IG, the first 12-year window is repositioning, not legislating.** Identify the marginalized IG whose agenda you actually want (Chonin here), and spend authority/characters/laws on raising their clout rather than burning attempts on doomed law swaps. The "marginalized IG must reach 5% before entering cabinet" threshold is the real first objective behind any visible reform journal entry. [ep01]

- **Time-limited "opening" modifiers are launch windows, not buffs to hoard.** When a one-shot modifier gives a 10-year strength boost to your reform IGs, fire it as early as possible so the entire window overlaps your law-change queue. [ep03] See ../laws-and-politics/passing-laws.md.

- **Compare malus duration to your strategic horizon before flinching at big negative numbers.** A -67% IG approval that decays in 5 years is cheaper than a permanent -44% blocker if you're playing an 80-year game. [ep03]

- **Sequence construction-cheapening before anything ambitious.** Logging camps → tooling workshop → iron mine → enable sawmill + iron-tools PMs. The build-cost drop compounds across every later project (~30% reduction inside one episode here). Co-locate input chains in one state when market access tech is weak — the state-vs-market price gap can wipe out the apparent profit. [ep02] See ../economy/construction.md and ../economy/building-balance-sheet.md.

- **Trim the army to the exact Power Projection threshold during forced-peace phases.** Battalions above the requirement are pure upkeep drain. [ep02]

- **Setbacks are worth spending on load-bearing laws.** A +10% pass chance on a law that unlocks further laws is a better trade than preserving a single character's popularity. Reserve setbacks defensively for late-cycle stalls where failure is near. [ep02, ep09] See ../laws-and-politics/passing-laws.md.

- **Pick the next law by who opposes it, not by what it does.** Look for laws where opposition movements are below the 25% activism threshold — those enact with no stall penalty. Movement activism must be above 25% for its support % to count as the law's success chance, so bolster the one movement that crossed it rather than spreading authority across several stuck below. [ep02, ep04, ep10] See ../laws-and-politics/political-movements.md.

- **Cultivate the movement you'll later need; bolster, don't grant clout.** Boosting Peasant Movement attraction now sets up Serfdom abolition later — political movements are tools, not nuisances. Bolster is reversible; clout grants are not. [ep02, ep10]

- **Stalled enactments are pre-determined per tick.** Reloading and re-clicking on the same in-game day yields the same roll. To change the outcome you must change the stall-chance modifier *before* the next monthly tick (negotiate, bribe an IG leader, accept costly demands), then advance time. [ep11]

- **For sway contests, find the cheapest interaction unlock and stack multiple low-cost pacts.** Don't build expensive infrastructure in the contested state if a Military Access + Money Transfer + Obligation pact yields the same monthly sway tick. Max the cash on obligation/transfer deals — the formula rewards maximum transferable amount. [ep03]

- **Don't trigger high-risk movement events at low legitimacy unless that's specifically what you want.** "Unforeseen consequences" warnings are a hard signal. But also: the same warning is the *desired* signal when you're trying to fork into a low-legitimacy-only branch (like Decree of Restoration over Taisei Hokan). Read which side you're on first. [ep03, ep07]

- **New tech = audit every PM it touches.** When a tech unlocks PMs (Lathes → dyes, lead glass, furniture-with-tools), walk the whole production chain: unlocked PM, building that consumes the new output, inputs you already produce locally. Free GDP is usually sitting one click away. But never switch a PM whose new input you don't yet produce — PM upgrades that require missing inputs are traps. [ep03, ep06]

- **Sequence consumable-dependent unit unlocks: build the input factory before retraining.** Ammunition, small arms, explosives must be online before swapping unit types, or organization/combat collapses on first engagement. [ep14, ep15] See ../military/army.md.

- **Build dedicated landing marines for naval invasions.** Every other infantry type carries -25% naval invasion efficiency; marines pay ~50% more goods upkeep for identical combat stats and zero penalty. Build them in states with local supply of the required goods to keep input costs low. [ep14, ep15] See ../military/war-and-naval-invasions.md.

- **Naval meta at game start: only build Ships of the Line, iron-reinforced hull (55 armor).** Frigates can't damage SOLs at this tier; 55 armor shuts down enemy medium guns. Maintenance/build cost premium is worth it. But crewing is a *separate* problem — Naval Administration must be built alongside (or before) shipyards or your fleet sits at low org. Shipyards have no input-shortage penalty, so hulls can be mass-produced before artillery/iron supply chains are ready. [ep05, ep06]

- **Match naval construction to the bottleneck you actually have.** If transport capacity for an upcoming invasion is the limit, optimize carrying-capacity-per-construction-week, not hull strength — cheaper frigates/cruisers win until troop-ship tech unlocks. Don't pay for speed above the fleet's slowest hull; new ships inherit the cap and points above are wasted. [ep13] See ../economy/shipbuilding.md and ../military/navy.md.

- **Coup mitigation without combat: unassign the rebelling general.** A general with no command produces no coup progress. Keep field armies split so no single commander has decisive force. The +coup-resistance church decision beats +coup-strength alternatives — asymmetric downside: a successful coup wipes law progress and locks the rebelling IG into government for ~20y. [ep06, ep08, ep11]

- **Audit government goods, not just market profitability.** Sort the state's own purchase line items; the top 1-2 goods (paper, iron, small arms, services) deserve dedicated state-built supply to crush their *price*, not to make profit. The saving on government spend dwarfs the building's own balance sheet. Decline events that raise pop demand for goods the state is a net buyer of. [ep13] See ../economy/treasury-and-deficit.md.

- **Treasury triage hierarchy under interest spiral:** pause construction first, redirect construction sectors to one PM tier, trim wages, raise consumption tax (legitimacy cost), sell government-owned buildings to private. Avoid more loans. Pausing state construction does NOT fix a deficit caused by private construction — private sector still consumes goods at market price. [ep07, ep09, ep13]

- **Unrecognized status = no debt tolerance.** 20-26% interest makes any borrowing destroy your budget. Recognition drops interest noticeably (here 26% → ~7.5%) — plan spending spikes for right after recognition. [ep07, ep11]

- **Lower local prices, don't import.** For institution inputs (paper) and SoL goods (clothing, groceries), distributing production across states beats relying on the world market — local production effects compound and avoid trade-route costs. One factory per state for consumer goods beats one mega-factory. [ep07, ep13, ep14] See ../economy/trade.md.

- **Privatize aggressively by default.** Frees treasury for construction, lets investment pool fund secondary industry. But foreign-invested companies leak dividends abroad — vet which country gets investment rights in your market. Foreign investment rights are still a cheap diplomatic currency when you have no alliance access; you can nationalize later. [ep02, ep06, ep08, ep09, ep16] See ../economy/foreign-investment.md.

- **Government Administrations are the urbanization speedrun building.** Cost ~250, build very fast, +20 urbanization per level, downgradable. When a journal requires urbanization tier 5 in a tight window, spam GAs across qualifying states and cancel the surplus after the threshold pings. Compare urbanization-per-construction-point, not per-level. [ep11, ep12]

- **Cabinet composition is a lever, not a default.** Deliberately exclude IGs that would radicalize, include all IGs that support the next law — even if it leaves the cabinet politically awkward elsewhere. Compose around the next law, not the last law. Keep individually-useful characters even when their IG is hostile. [ep10, ep11]

- **When forced to concede, pick the law that satisfies the maximum number of active movements.** Census Suffrage defused Liberal *and* Radical movements simultaneously here. Don't enact maximum-tier when minimum-tier in the same direction also defuses — the movement wants the direction, not the cap. [ep14, ep16] See ../laws-and-politics/political-movements.md.

- **Restoration / unique national journals have implicit law prerequisites that are not obvious from the journal text.** Warrior Caste swap requires Lex Edo Status, which requires fighting the dominant landed IG. Pre-check law prerequisites and chain them in the queue, not reactively. [ep09, ep10]

- **Run the journal-entry clock and the law-change clock in parallel from day one.** A 12-year restoration timer assumes you're already enacting, building, and researching simultaneously — sequential play will miss the window. [ep08]

- **State Religion is a reversible tool, not a permanent identity.** Adopting it to satisfy a journal and start a conversion threshold is fine — plan the unwind law for after the threshold is met. [ep11]

- **Power Blocs can be formed without vassals.** The Sovereign Empire archetype works for a recognized-or-soon-recognized major with surplus influence. Don't assume the prerequisite you remember is still required — re-check the formation tooltip. Defer activation until you can absorb the -60 relations hit forming gives other GPs. [ep09, ep11] See ../diplomacy/power-blocs.md.

- **Protectorate-first expansion against weak neighbours** gives income, a power-bloc member, and a path to later annexation at lower infamy than direct conquest. Threats often back down with zero fighting. But you can't protect a subject while tied up in another diplomatic play — sequence subject-creation wars so you have free bandwidth during the truce window. [ep16, ep17] See ../diplomacy/small-nation-play.md.

- **For journal-entry forks, prefer permanent rewards over time-limited modifiers.** Assimilation, building slots, arable land, new crops, institution unlocks all beat loyalty/research-speed buffs that expire. [ep08, ep14, ep16]

- **Election meddling can flip an IG to opposition.** Using "favor party" risks pushing the meddled IG out of government entirely if other coalition IGs object — leaving you with an illegitimate government and no path to enact laws. Don't meddle while mid-enactment of a controversial law. Consecutive meddles compound penalties. [ep12, ep14, ep16]

- **Per Capita Taxation has a peasant-share crossover point (~70%).** Below it, land tax + consumption is fine. Above it (enough pops in non-peasant buildings), per-capita yields more and relieves peasant SoL during war. Persisting through enactment failures is correct once the crossover is reached. [ep13, ep15]

- **Patch 1.13.1 literacy decay: literacy now decays toward education access.** If literacy falls without an obvious cause, raise education access (Promote Social Mobility, schooling laws, religious-academia institutions) — the previous "literacy is sticky" intuition is obsolete. [ep13]

- **Save before declaring wars on novel targets.** State-region ownership (the state your war goal sits in may be a different tag's territory), garrison count, naval supply throughput, and strait adjacency all need verification. "Weak navy" does not imply "weak coastal defence." [ep14]

- **Heir interest group matters.** A modernizer/Gosho heir compounds when they take the throne (+25% prominence to their IG on succession). Sudden ruler death can reset your IG legitimacy and force a re-think of which laws are passable — expect to lose mid-enactment laws when the throne changes. [ep15]

- **Admiral death drains fleet supply.** Always dock to recover rather than waiting at sea. Admirals die often in the late game — plan for re-embarkation between wars. [ep15, ep16]

- **Company picks: optimize for input-goods your economy bleeds on, not for raw output bonuses.** Mitsubishi (paper/iron) and Mitsui (clothing/dyes/silk) directly hit Japan's pain points. Wait for prerequisites rather than burning a slot on a mediocre default. [ep17] See ../economy/companies.md.

- **Mass unemployment with no employable buildings is a danger sign.** Building construction must keep pace with pop growth, or radicals spiral past any law/edict you can throw at them. Once a pop class is mass-radicalized it self-sustains regardless of SoL interventions. [ep17] See ../pops/standard-of-living.md.

## Where this contradicts (or refines) the tutorials

- **"Always promote marginalized IGs to cabinet when they cross 5%"** — refined. The campaign explicitly *rejected* promoting Chonin to cabinet the moment they hit Influential because it would have flipped the powerful Daimyo from neutral to angry, doubling stall chance from 25% to 50% [ep02]. The general rule "promote eligible reformers" needs the caveat: stall chance is multiplicative, and a neutral powerful IG is often worth more than the IG-strength gain from promoting a newly-eligible one. See ../laws-and-politics/passing-laws.md.

- **"Build only Ships of the Line at this tech tier"** — contradicted in the late game. The naval-doctrine page advises SOL-only because frigates can't damage SOLs. The campaign *abandoned* this rule once transport capacity for the Korea invasion became the binding constraint, pivoting construction to frigates and then cruisers because carrying-capacity-per-construction-week mattered more than hull dominance [ep13]. The lesson: the SOL-only rule is correct for fleet battles, wrong when the limiting factor is sealift. See ../military/navy.md and ../economy/shipbuilding.md.

- **"Save-scumming irreversible buttons is poor form"** — refined. The campaign *intentionally* save-scummed the Imperial Restoration click to test which legitimacy state produced which branch (Taisei Hokan vs Decree of Restoration), then deliberately tanked legitimacy and re-clicked [ep07]. Similarly the Sonno Joi crisis was save-scummed to land the Imperial victory at the high success-chance tick [ep08]. The tutorial advice "play it as it lies" oversimplifies — for branched buttons whose outcome depends on hidden state, branch-testing via save is a legitimate decision technique.

- **"Stalled enactments are random and you can't do much"** — directly contradicted. The campaign discovered stall outcomes are pre-determined per tick: reloading and re-clicking the same in-game day yields the same roll [ep11]. The only lever is changing the stall-chance modifier *before* the next monthly tick. The tutorial framing of "stalls as random setbacks" misses the underlying determinism. See ../laws-and-politics/passing-laws.md.

- **"Power Blocs require vassals/subjects to form"** — outdated. The Sovereign Empire archetype was formed with zero vassals once influence was sufficient [ep09]. Always re-check the formation tooltip rather than relying on a remembered prerequisite. See ../diplomacy/power-blocs.md.

- **"Patronize pops with authority to build IG prominence"** — refined as bad value. The campaign measured the effect at ~0.1-0.2% prominence per 100 Authority [ep04] and abandoned the technique except in emergencies, redirecting authority to decrees, social mobility, and bolstering movements. The tutorial advice to "use authority on character/pop patronage" undersells how poor the conversion rate is.

- **"Literacy is sticky and decays slowly"** — obsoleted by patch 1.13.1. Literacy now decays toward education access; if literacy falls without an obvious cause, the fix is education access (Promote Social Mobility, schooling laws), not waiting [ep13]. See ../pops/standard-of-living.md.

- **"Government Administrations are for bureaucracy capacity"** — refined into a dual use. The campaign discovered GAs are the *urbanization speedrun building* (cost ~250, +20 urbanization per level, downgradable), beating Universities and Art Academies on construction-per-urbanization-point [ep11, ep12]. The tutorial framing of GA as a bureaucracy building only misses their use as a journal-deadline throughput tool.
