# Case Study: Restoring the Spanish Empire as Cuba (Tarkusarkusar)

## Overview

A ~28-minute single-video campaign by Tarkusarkusar (uploaded 2025-09-04, patch unspecified, presumed 1.10–1.11 era) playing 1836 Cuba into a Hispanic-revival great power. The arc is short and instructive: start as a Spanish colony with ~3M GDP, no construction sector, and a "cruel" governor; build a plantation-led economy; gain Dominion status without losing the Spanish market; reconquer the former Spanish-American empire piece-by-piece; overreach on Britain and lose; pivot to coffee colonialism in West/East Africa to save the budget; transition to Council Republic; lose to Britain a second time; accept the power-bloc legacy. The run is openly an [autobiographical] showcase rather than an optimized run — but precisely because the player narrates *why* each decision was made, it leaves more usable rules of thumb than a polished playthrough would.

The video deliberately includes two failed wars with Britain, a misdiagnosed mid-game budget crisis, and a save-reload, all of which are presented as teaching moments rather than cut from the recording.

## Phase 1 — Plantation island and the Dominion war (1836–1851)

**Starting position.** Cuba is a Spanish colony under Miguel Tacón ("cruel" trait — −SoL across the board, but consumption-tax authority cost reduced [[01:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=64s)]). Ten construction points base, no construction sector. The strategic objective for the first decade is *not* industry — it's pop count, SoL, and migration attraction so that by the time the dominion war fires, the economy can absorb the shock.

**Coffee, specifically.** Of Cuba's plantation goods, only coffee has a prestige-good company [[02:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=125s)]. A tobacco/cigar play would have been thematic but mechanically inferior. *Plantation choice should be picked by which prestige company you can later run, not by raw price.*

**Why plantations beat industry under tight CP.** A coffee plantation is 200 CP for ~5K jobs; a steel mill is 800 CP for ~5K jobs. In the eighty weeks it takes to put down one steel mill you can put down four plantations and twenty thousand jobs [[02:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=155s)]. More jobs → more SoL → more migration → more pops to staff the eventual industrial build. The lever isn't profit per building, it's pops created per construction-week.

**Skip the first construction sector if your investment pool has nothing to feed it.** Building one construction sector takes you from 10 CP → 15 CP, but the private sector immediately claims half (7.5 to you, 7.5 to the investment pool) [[03:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=216s)]. Until the IP has profitable industries to spend its half on, that 7.5 is wasted and you're net-down from your original 10. The player's rule: *don't build construction sectors until you can afford 2–3 at once, AND have profitable plantations / companies that the IP will want to expand.* (Verify in current patch — the exact private/state split depends on Economic System law; the principle is independent of the exact ratio.)

**Migration decree + minimum taxes as an explicit pop pump.** Drop taxes to attract Spanish migrants while still a colony [[04:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=246s)]. The "lost" tax revenue is bought back later in pops, SoL, and IG strength — and the Americans can be milked for diplomatic-pact money to cover the gap.

**Cuban Freedom Congress (independence-support obligation).** Identified the Americans as belligerent toward Spain and made a treaty: obligation in exchange for independence support, cash transfer, and military support [[04:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=246s)]. £700/month "isn't insane but is a nice boost" early — and crucially, *it's renegotiable upward*. When Spain dragged Cuba into a war against the US, breaking the treaty, the post-war Cuban Liberation Congress paid more [[07:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=460s)]. **Treaty rupture as leverage** — when your overlord forces a treaty break, the next version of the same treaty typically pays better.

**Out-of-infrastructure halts the investment pool, not the state queue.** Around 1842 the budget visibly collapses — the IP stopped contributing because Cuba hit its infrastructure cap. All construction was now coming out of the government [[06:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=399s)]. *Fix is railways, not less construction.* Ports work but pay back slowly (merchant marine + nothing else). Wait the railway research timer out and resume.

**Dominion before independence.** The 1849 war wasn't for full independence — it was for Dominion status [[08:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=522s)]. Dominion gains independent diplomacy and freedom from forced war participation while keeping access to Spain's market and avoiding the unrecognized-power interest-rate penalty. Independence proper waits until you're recognized-power-ready. Failed sub-goal: as a subject you can't nationalize buildings owned by your overlord [[09:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=553s)] — check before banking on it.

**War-budget triage as a routine.** During the war the budget cratered (military upkeep + construction). The player accepted it as a one-time cost; the alternative — pausing all construction until the war ended — would have meant delaying every downstream timer (railways, companies, voting reform) [[09:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=553s)]. *Wars are budget shocks you plan for, not crises to triage.*

## Phase 2 — The Cuban reconquista (1851–1867)

**Sequential annexations of weak Hispanic states.** Honduras → Nicaragua → Costa Rica → Venezuela → Haiti → New Granada → independence from Spain. Central America in particular has neither resources nor population, but as a small power you take what you can get [[15:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=917s)]. The pattern: pick targets one notch below your weight class with no great-power patron, conquer fast, repeat.

**Brazil as the recurring spoiler.** Venezuela war: Brazil intervened, outgunned Cuba on land. The player won via naval dominance + an enlistment decree + high military wages to keep reinforcement flowing [[11:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=674s)]. *Naval dominance lets you brute-force land disadvantages by reinforcing on the front line while the enemy can't.* When Brazil returned for New Granada (1862), the player's army was caught underprepared and stalemated — won eventually only because the home islands were safe under naval supremacy [[12:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=734s)].

**Company picks: throughput on a chokepoint, not flashy output.** The Cuban Metal company increases iron-mine throughput, which decreases the construction-goods price line — saving more on a heavy build queue than its own profit shows [[10:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=644s)]. Same logic as the Japan case study's Mitsubishi pick: optimize companies for the input chain you bleed on. See [Companies](../economy/companies.md).

**Annex Haiti when their liberty desire becomes a free puppet.** After Venezuela, Cuba annexed Haiti partly to remove the liberty-desire bookkeeping [[11:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=704s)] — subjects you can't easily protect become a tax on your attention; clean them up when convenient.

**Independence war designed around Spain's overcommitment.** July 1866 — declared with humiliation + independence war goals, expecting a slow war. Spain instead committed a huge army to defend Mexico, which the US then slaughtered, collapsing Spanish war support [[13:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=795s)]. War ended Sept 1867 in one year. *The "what's the enemy's worst-case forced commitment" question is sometimes more useful than counting battalions.*

## Phase 3 — Imperial overreach and the Britain wall (1867–1885)

**Recognition → great power → debt paid off.** Recognized power immediately on independence; debt cleared by summer 1870 [[14:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=887s)]. The 26% → ~7.5% interest-rate drop that recognition gives (per the Japan case study) is the implicit reason Cuba could spend its way through the reconquista years and still come out positive.

**Voting laws and the emperor process.** Wealth voting [[14:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=857s)] → opens the candidate pool → enact monarchy with whichever IG leader wins. The lesson is sequencing: *use the voting law to change who the emperor will be* rather than treating monarchy as a binary toggle.

**The mid-game budget crisis (1876) is structural, not tactical.** "Too much construction for how little investment I generate" [[16:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=979s)]. Beyond coffee, Cuba had no high-profit industry feeding the investment pool; the government was funding every project itself. **Profits don't matter early; they matter the moment you have 3+ construction sectors.** The diagnostic: divide your weekly investment-pool inflow by your weekly construction-good cost. If the IP can't cover its share, you don't have a *construction* problem — you have a *profitable industry* problem.

**Power Bloc as Sovereign Empire.** Novomundo formed in 1880 with zero vassals (the Japan run already established this is supported by the Sovereign Empire archetype). Rivaled Britain and allied the US [[18:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1100s)].

**Britain war attempt #1 — Market Capitalist = the whole nation is overseas.** Under Market Capitalism *every* internal route counts as a convoy route. If Britain destroys your Atlantic convoys (moving goods between Cuba and your African plantations), the disruption also cuts market access from Cuba to Panama and the rest of your American holdings [[19:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1161s)]. Against the world's largest navy this is unwinnable. Save-reloaded [[19:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1192s)]. **Rule:** *don't rival Britain on Market Capitalism without a continental land base.* (Verify in current patch — Economic System overhaul has shifted the convoy/overseas mechanics between releases.)

**Mexico as consolation invasion.** When the big play fails, weakening Britain's pact-mates can still be net-positive [[20:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1223s)]. The player concedes in narration that it was "pretty pointless" — file under stress-relief moves that don't move the needle.

## Phase 4 — Coffee colonialism and the Council Republic (1885–1905)

**The coffee colonialism trick.** Invade Zulu, Portugal (for the Mozambique coast), Benin, Sokoto for unused arable land [[20:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1253s), [21:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1283s)]. Leave the conquered states unincorporated and build coffee plantations:

- Unincorporated → discriminated pops → wages collapse → plantation profits explode.
- Unincorporated → no government taxes, but also no need — the **investment pool funds construction itself**.
- Violence suppression + road maintenance decrease accelerates the colonial build queue.
- The prestige-goods company you set up in Phase 1 now buys these plantations on the open market.

By the time this finishes, the player went from a deficit budget at 50M GDP (1884) to a balanced budget at 95M GDP (Dec 1891) [[22:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1344s)], without raising any taxes — pure investment-pool repair via low-wage colonial plantations.

This pattern is **the single most generalizable lesson in the video**: when your mid-game budget is broken because IP < construction cost, low-wage colonial plantations under your prestige-goods company are the fastest IP repair available. The state extracts nothing directly (no taxes on unincorporated) but is freed from funding its own construction.

**Tariff trade-off explicitly demonstrated.** Putting a tariff on exported coffee yielded ~£60K/month tariff income but caused the home trade center to *lose* £44K/month [[22:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1375s)]. The trade center then drops its volume, GDP falls. The video frames it as "I care about budget more than GDP right now" — **tariffs are a budget tool, not a growth tool; apply them when treasury > GDP in your priority stack.** See [Trade](../economy/trade.md).

**Colonial administration release as a tax-extraction upgrade.** Release a colonial administration over your unincorporated coffee provinces. Two effects [[23:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1406s)]:

1. **Immediate incorporation** of the released states (no more uninncorporated mappy mouse), unlocking taxation and tariffs on their workers and trade.
2. **Tribute from the subject** — they collect taxes, you receive a cut.

You trade direct control for tax efficiency. Player flags this as "not always best" without specifying when — likely when arable-land conquest is ongoing and you'd lose the unincorporated wage-suppression effect mid-development. *Wait until the plantations are mostly built, then release.*

**Council Republic transition compromise.** Picked Commercial Agriculture as the agrarian compromise rather than Collectivized Agriculture [[26:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1560s)]. Reasoning: *Cuba had little farming to collectivize* (plantations don't count the same way), so the collective payoff was small while the political cost (upsetting the rich/moderate/radical balance) was real. **Match the compromise law to the size of the sector it modifies — collectivizing 5% of your economy is not worth the destabilization.** See [Slavery Abolition](../laws-and-politics/slavery-abolition.md) for a similar coalition-management read.

**Workers Republic anarchist path.** Per the player, "aside from multiculturalism, total trash" reward-wise [[26:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1591s)]. If you're not roleplaying, pick the communist branch.

**Britain war attempt #2 (1905) — America joined Britain.** Demonstrates that *alliance loyalty isn't durable across the game's lifespan*. Re-check pact members before each major war [[27:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1623s)]. Lost predictably; the run ended with the power-bloc consolation prize.

## Reusable lessons

These are the rules of thumb the campaign generates that don't depend on playing Cuba specifically.

### Construction & economy

- **Plantations-first beats industry-first while CP is tight.** Optimize pops-created-per-construction-week, not profit-per-building. Stay on plantations until at least one prestige-goods company is running and infrastructure is past the cap. [[02:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=155s)] See [Construction](../economy/construction.md) and [Building Balance Sheet](../economy/building-balance-sheet.md).

- **Picking plantation crops by prestige-company availability.** Cuba could have done tobacco/cigars thematically but coffee was the only plantation good with a prestige-company prerequisite available. Pick the input chain whose endgame multiplier exists in the company list. [[02:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=125s)] See [Companies](../economy/companies.md).

- **Skip the first construction sector if the investment pool has nothing to feed it.** Building 1 sector activates the private/state CP split — if the private side has no profitable industries to expand, you're net-down. Wait until you can afford 2–3 sectors *and* have IP-attractive industry. [[03:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=216s), [04:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=246s)] (Verify split ratio in current patch.)

- **Out-of-infrastructure is an investment-pool stall, not a state-queue stall.** Sudden budget collapse mid-construction usually means the IP halted at the infra cap and all CP is now state-funded. Fix is railways. [[06:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=399s)] See [Construction](../economy/construction.md).

- **Profits matter on a delay.** Early game: pop count + SoL drive everything. The moment you have 3+ construction sectors and a fat queue, the investment pool needs profit-rich industries or you'll be paying out of your treasury. The diagnostic: weekly IP inflow ÷ weekly construction-good cost. [[16:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=979s)] See [Foreign Investment](../economy/foreign-investment.md).

- **Coffee-colonialism budget repair.** When mid-game IP is starving construction, conquer or colonize a low-wage region, leave it unincorporated, and build plantations under your prestige-goods company. The wage gap explodes profits → IP swells → state stops paying for construction. Violence suppression + road maintenance decrease accelerates the build queue. [[20:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1253s), [21:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1283s)] See [Colonization](../diplomacy/colonization.md).

- **Colonial-administration release = incorporate-and-tribute.** Once the plantations are mostly built, releasing a colonial administration over them immediately incorporates the states (unlocking taxes and tariffs) and converts to a tribute relationship. Wait until the unincorporated wage-suppression isn't doing useful work anymore. [[23:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1406s)] (Verify exact mechanic in current patch.) See [Colonization](../diplomacy/colonization.md).

- **Tariffs are a budget knob, not a growth knob.** Putting a tariff on your own export raises tariff income but the home trade center loses on that route and drops its volume — net GDP down. Apply when the budget is your dominant priority, not when you're trying to grow. [[22:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1375s)] See [Trade](../economy/trade.md).

### Diplomacy & war

- **Dominion before independence — keep the market, lose the obligations.** A Dominion war buys you independent diplomacy and freedom from forced war participation while keeping you in your overlord's market and avoiding the unrecognized-power interest-rate penalty. Independence proper waits for great-power readiness. [[08:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=522s)] See [Small-Nation Play](../diplomacy/small-nation-play.md).

- **Treaty rupture is upside.** If your overlord drags you into a war that breaks a foreign treaty, the renegotiated version typically pays better. Don't mourn the broken pact — re-sign at a higher number. [[07:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=460s)]

- **Subjects can't nationalize overlord-owned buildings.** Check before banking on it as a post-independence revenue source. [[09:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=553s)] See [Foreign Investment](../economy/foreign-investment.md).

- **Naval dominance brute-forces land disadvantage if your reinforcement chain is intact.** Enlistment decree + high military wages keep the front fed; the enemy without naval access can't match. [[11:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=674s)] See [Army](../military/army.md), [War & Naval Invasions](../military/war-and-naval-invasions.md).

- **Don't rival Britain on Market Capitalism without a continental land base.** Under Market Capitalism every internal trade route counts as a convoy route, so convoy raiding by a major navy severs your domestic economy as well as your overseas one. [[19:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1161s)] (Verify in current patch — Economic System mechanics have shifted between releases.) See [Trade](../economy/trade.md), [Power Blocs](../diplomacy/power-blocs.md).

- **Re-check alliance composition before each major war.** Allies who joined your side in war #1 may join the *other* side in war #2 — alliances aren't durable across the game's lifespan. [[27:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1623s)]

### Political reform

- **Use voting law to change who the next monarch will be.** Wealth → Census → Universal each changes which IG leaders are emperor-eligible. Treat the sequence as a cast list, not a binary toggle. [[14:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=857s), [15:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=948s)] See [Passing Laws](../laws-and-politics/passing-laws.md).

- **Match the compromise law to the size of the sector it modifies.** Council Republic compromise: Commercial Agriculture beat Collectivized when Cuba's farming sector was small — the collective payoff didn't justify the coalition stress. [[26:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1560s)] See [Political Movements](../laws-and-politics/political-movements.md).

## Where this contradicts (or refines) the wiki

- **"Build construction sectors as soon as you can afford one"** — refined. The campaign explicitly delayed the first sector until 3 could be built simultaneously and the IP had profitable industries to feed. The state/private split makes a single sector *net negative* on usable state CP if the IP allocation is wasted. [[03:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=216s)] See [Construction](../economy/construction.md).

- **"Plantations are early-game training wheels you graduate from"** — refined. Plantations remain the IP-repair tool in mid-game as long as low-wage unincorporated land exists. The "graduate to industry" rule applies to your *home* economy; colonial plantations stay forever. [[17:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1070s), [20:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1253s)] See [Construction](../economy/construction.md), [Colonization](../diplomacy/colonization.md).

- **"Tariffs raise revenue at the cost of consumer prices"** — refined. The bigger cost is to your *own* exporters: the home trade center on a tariffed route loses money and drops volume, so tariff income partly comes out of your own GDP. Frame tariffs as a *redistribution from exporters to treasury*, not a free revenue source. [[22:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1375s)] See [Trade](../economy/trade.md).

- **"Independence first, expand second"** — refined. The Dominion intermediate step buys nearly all the benefits of independence (own diplomacy, no forced wars) at none of the cost (lost market access, recognition interest penalty). Independence proper is the *last* step, after you're great-power ready. [[08:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=522s)] See [Small-Nation Play](../diplomacy/small-nation-play.md).

- **"Power Blocs are diplomatic infrastructure to protect what you have"** — extended. They're also identity statements — Novomundo as a Sovereign Empire framed Cuba's reconquista narratively. Mechanically the same; politically the framing matters for which targets feel legitimate. [[18:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8&t=1100s)] See [Power Blocs](../diplomacy/power-blocs.md).
