# Case Study: Restoring the Spanish Empire as Cuba

**Source:** [Restoring the SPANISH EMPIRE as CUBA - Victoria 3 GIGACHAD STORY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKZwT9G7Dq8) by Tarkusarkusar, 2025-09-04, 28:32. Patch unspecified (presumed 1.10–1.11 era). Notes: [01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md).

**TL;DR:** A ~28-minute single-video campaign that takes 1836 Cuba — a Spanish colony with ten construction points, no construction sector, a cruel governor, and three million pounds of GDP — to a ~100M-GDP Council Republic ruling a Hispanic-Atlantic power bloc and an East-and-West African coffee empire. The arc has four moves: a plantation-led pop pump until the Dominion war can be fought; a fifteen-year reconquista across Central America and Northern South America; a mid-game budget collapse that turns into the most generalizable lesson in the video; and two failed wars against Britain that resolve into accepting the power bloc, not the formal empire, as the legacy. The video is openly unoptimized — the failures are narrated rather than cut — which is why it teaches more than a polished run would.

## Setting the stage

Cuba opens in 1836 as a colony of the Spanish Empire, ruled by Miguel Tacón. Tacón carries the *cruel* trait, which lowers consumption-tax authority cost but drops the standard of living across the board; he is also Spanish-born and only happened to be in Cuba for the four years that included game-start, so the player will outlive him politically inside the first decade. The strategic given is that Cuba has natural resources, modest population, and almost no industrial base — ten construction points, no sector, a single tooling-and-iron chain to build from scratch, and one obvious export crop (coffee) with which a plantation economy can be jump-started.

The standard subject-play assumption — sit tight, build, wait for an independence opportunity — does not fit the player's stated long-term goal of replacing Spain rather than escaping it. Replacing Spain requires great-power status, which requires recognition, which requires independence, which requires either a long autonomy ladder or a war Cuba can win. So the first decade has to be about pop count and SoL (to make migration attractive and the eventual war affordable) rather than about visible journal goals. The reformer-side bet — Spanish migrants, plantation profits, an obligation-for-independence-support pact with the Americans — is set on day one and then defended as the budget allows it.

## Narrative

The early years are construction-shaped by tight CP. Plantations rather than steel mills, because plantations are 200 construction points for ~5K jobs and a steel mill is 800 for the same headcount; in the eighty weeks it takes to put down one steel mill, the player puts down four coffee plantations and twenty thousand jobs, which is twenty thousand more pops slowly drifting toward a tolerable SoL and thus toward attracting Spanish migrants. Coffee specifically, not the other plantation goods, because coffee is the only Cuban plantation crop with a prestige-goods company — the endgame multiplier exists in the company list, where tobacco and cigars would have been thematic but mechanically unsupported. Taxes drop to the floor to make migration attractive; the gap in revenue is covered by an obligation-for-cash treaty with the Americans (Cuban Freedom Congress), trading a diplomatic obligation for independence-support, a money transfer, and military access. The first construction sector is *not* built immediately, which is the first surprising choice — one sector would push CP from 10 to 15 but immediately ceded half to the investment pool, and the IP has nothing yet to invest in, so a single sector is net-negative on usable state CP. Three sectors are built at once in 1839 when the budget can afford them and the plantations are starting to give the IP somewhere productive to spend [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

A mid-1840s budget collapse looks like a spending problem but is actually an infrastructure problem: Cuba has hit its infrastructure cap, the investment pool has stopped contributing to construction at all, and the state queue is paying for everything by itself. The fix is railways — ports were considered and rejected because they pay back too slowly — and the player simply pauses construction until research finishes, accepts a small loan, and resumes once one rail line lifts the cap. Around the same time, Spain drags Cuba into a war against the Americans, which breaks the Cuban Freedom Congress treaty. The renegotiated version (Cuban Liberation Congress) pays more than the original. The 1849 war is then explicitly *not* for independence — it is for Dominion status, which buys independent diplomacy and freedom from forced participation in overlord wars while preserving Spanish market access and avoiding the unrecognized-power interest-rate penalty. Two parallel naval landings in Haiti shortcut what would have been a long grind through the combined Spanish-Haitian armies, and the war ends in April 1851 with Cuba a dominion [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

The reconquista runs from 1851 to 1867. Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, and Haiti fall easily — small Hispanic states with no great-power patron — and the harder targets, Venezuela and New Granada, are each contested by Brazil, who outguns Cuba on land. Both wars are won via the same trick: naval dominance plus an enlistment decree plus high military wages, which reinforce battalions on the front faster than Brazil can match. The Cuban Metal company is picked in 1855 not for its profit but for the iron-throughput buff, which lowers the construction-goods price line and so saves more on the build queue than it shows on its own balance sheet. The independence war against Spain (July 1866) adds a humiliation goal alongside independence; Spain commits a huge army to defend Mexico, the Americans destroy it, and Spain surrenders in September 1867. Cuba is independent, hits Great Power in January 1868, and clears its national debt by summer 1870 [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

The mid-game stalls around 1876 into a budget crisis that is the run's most instructive moment. Construction is heavy, the queue is full, and the player has finally figured out the diagnosis: too much construction for how little the investment pool generates. Beyond coffee plantations, Cuba has no high-profit industry, so the IP can't pay its share of construction and the government is funding everything itself. *Profits don't matter early; they matter the moment you have three construction sectors.* The fix is coffee colonialism — invading the Zulu Kingdom, Portuguese Mozambique, Benin, and Sokoto for unused arable land, leaving the conquered states unincorporated so wages collapse, and building coffee plantations there for the prestige-goods company to scoop up. Profits explode, the IP swells, and the state stops paying for construction. The budget goes from a 50M-GDP deficit in 1884 to balanced at 95M GDP by December 1891. A tariff on coffee exports follows — explicitly framed as a budget trade rather than a growth move, because the home trade center on tariffed routes drops volume and part of the tariff income comes out of Cuba's own GDP. A colonial administration is then released over the plantation states, which incorporates them immediately and converts the relationship to one paying tribute. Two attempts to attack Britain — one save-reloaded after convoy raiding cuts internal market access under Market Capitalism, one accepted as the ending after the Americans switch sides — establish the run's limit. The Council Republic transitions through Commercial Agriculture (March 1899) and Cooperative Ownership (September 1904), explicitly skipping Collectivized Farming because Cuba's farming sector is too small for the collective payoff to justify the coalition stress. The run ends on 100M GDP, a balanced budget, and the Novomundo power bloc that resembles, if not actually restores, the old Spanish Empire [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

## Key decisions

- **Build only coffee plantations early, not industry or any other plantation good.** The alternative was a tobacco/cigars play, thematically richer for a Caribbean run, or skipping straight to a tools-and-iron industrial base. Tobacco was rejected because no prestige-goods company exists for it — the endgame multiplier is missing from the company list. Industry was rejected on the cost-per-job math: 200 CP for ~5K plantation jobs vs 800 CP for the same headcount in a steel mill, so four plantations fit in the time of one steel mill. Reasoning: in the first decade the lever is pops created per construction-week, because pops drive SoL drive migration; profit per building only becomes load-bearing later [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Defer the first construction sector until the budget can build three at once.** The alternative — the default-tutorial move — was to put down a single sector as soon as cash allowed it, going from 10 base CP to 15. Rejected because a single sector activates the private/state CP split (here 7.5 / 7.5) with nothing yet for the IP to spend its half on, so the state's *usable* CP drops from 10 to 7.5 and the IP allocation is wasted. The single-sector posture is net-negative. Three sectors flip the math: state share is 12.5 (above the original 10), IP share is 12.5 with plantations now able to absorb it, and the queue moves faster across the board [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Drop taxes to the floor and run on American obligation cash instead.** The alternative was a normal-tax posture funding the state with its own revenue. Rejected because Cuba's only path to scale was Spanish migration, and migration responds to SoL, which responds to taxes. The Cuban Freedom Congress treaty (obligation for independence-support + cash + military access) covered the revenue gap while migration ran. Reasoning: tax revenue from a small pop base is small in absolute terms; the same cash from a great power obligation is fungible and the pact also seeded the independence-support diplomacy [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Fight for Dominion in 1849 rather than for full independence.** The alternative was to declare independence outright once enough army existed to fight Spain. Rejected because independence at that point would have meant losing Spanish market access (Cuba was still a coffee exporter into it), paying the unrecognized-power interest-rate penalty, and fighting Spain alone before American support could be cashed in. Dominion gave the diplomatic upside (own wars, own treaties, no forced participation) without the economic and political downside. Independence proper was sequenced behind great-power readiness instead [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Pause construction during the 1846–1848 infrastructure crunch rather than cancel projects or cut wages.** The alternative was to cancel half the queue or slash wages to balance the budget. Rejected because the underlying problem wasn't overspending — it was that Cuba had hit its infrastructure cap and the investment pool had gone idle, leaving the state funding everything alone. Pausing freezes the bleed without surrendering progress; the queue resumes the day railway research completes and one line opens the cap. Reasoning: distinguish "too much construction" (a real problem) from "infrastructure has stalled the IP" (a different problem with a different fix) [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Pick the Cuban Metal company for the iron-throughput buff, not for its profit.** The alternative was a higher-profit consumer-goods company. Rejected because Cuba's construction queue was the binding constraint and iron mines feed the construction-goods price line; lower construction-goods price compounds across every later project the same way the Japan run used Mitsubishi. Reasoning: companies optimize for the input chain you bleed on, not for their own balance-sheet profit [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Add humiliation to the independence wargoal vs Spain.** The alternative was a clean independence-only war. Reasoning: humiliation deepens the war-support hit on Spain and creates an additional path to a quick capitulation if Spain over-commits. In this run the play worked indirectly — Spain stuffed Mexico with troops the Americans then slaughtered, and war support collapsed inside a year. Independence-only would still have won eventually; humiliation was the bet on a *faster* win [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Diagnose the 1876 budget crisis as a profits problem, not a spending problem.** The intuitive read was to cut construction. Rejected because the queue itself was producing GDP, jobs, and journal progress; the actual issue was that beyond coffee, Cuba had no industry profitable enough to feed the investment pool, so the IP couldn't pay its share and the state funded the queue alone. Reasoning: the diagnostic is weekly IP inflow vs weekly construction-good cost. If IP < cost, the fix is more profitable industry, not less construction [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Solve the budget crisis with unincorporated colonial plantations rather than tax raises or austerity.** The alternative was raising taxes or cutting wages on the home economy. Rejected because either would damage SoL and migration, the two pillars the early-game posture was built on. Instead, conquer low-wage land in Africa, leave it unincorporated so wages collapse further, and build the prestige-goods company's coffee plantations there. Profits explode, the IP swells, the state stops funding construction. Violence suppression and road maintenance decrees accelerate the colonial build queue. Reasoning: an unincorporated plantation in a discriminated region pays zero direct taxes but contributes to the IP, which is exactly what a state-side budget crisis needs [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Release a colonial administration over the African plantation states once the build was mostly complete.** The alternative was to keep the states unincorporated indefinitely (preserves wage suppression) or to incorporate them directly (loses the wage suppression in exchange for tax control). The colonial-administration release path is a third option: the subject's states are incorporated immediately on release, so taxes and tariffs now work, and tribute is remitted to the overlord. Reasoning: the unincorporated wage-suppression had finished doing useful work (plantations are built), so the next-best use of the same territory was tax efficiency through tribute [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Save-reload the first Britain war rather than play it out.** The alternative was to accept the loss and rebuild. Rejected because the loss vector — convoy raiding under Market Capitalism severing internal market access between non-adjacent states (Cuba ↔ Panama, Cuba ↔ African plantations) — was structural, not tactical. Playing it out would have stripped years of plantation development off the map. Reasoning: when the defeat condition is a law-level structural mismatch rather than a tactical error, the rerun is information; the second attempt simply doesn't pick that fight again [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

- **Pick Commercial Agriculture as the Council Republic compromise, not Collectivized Farming.** The alternative was the more ideologically pure collectivization. Rejected because Cuba's farming sector was small relative to its plantations and industry — collectivizing 5% of an economy is not worth the political cost of upsetting the rich-moderate-radical balance needed to pass cooperative ownership next. Reasoning: match a compromise law to the size of the sector it modifies, not to its ideological flavor [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/cuba-spanish-restoration/01-restoring-spanish-empire-cuba.md)].

## Reusable lessons

- **Construction sectors have an inflection point: one is worse than zero, three is better than one.** A lone sector activates the private/state CP split with nothing for the private side to do, so the state ends up with less usable CP than before. Three sectors flip the math because the investment pool has enough share to feed something useful and the state share is also larger in absolute terms. Defer the first sector until you can build three.

- **Mid-game budget collapses are usually an investment-pool problem, not a spending problem.** The diagnostic is weekly IP inflow vs weekly construction-good cost. If IP can't pay its share, the fix is more profitable industry, not less construction. Cutting the queue removes the symptom and leaves the cause.

- **Coffee colonialism is the IP-repair move.** Conquer or colonize low-wage land, leave it unincorporated, build prestige-goods-company plantations there. Wage collapse → profit explosion → IP swells → state stops paying for construction. Violence suppression and road maintenance decrees accelerate the build queue. The trick generalizes to any prestige-goods plantation crop; coffee is just Cuba's case.

- **Dominion is the cheap intermediate between subject and independent.** It buys independent diplomacy and freedom from forced wars while preserving market access and dodging the unrecognized-power interest penalty. Use it when full independence would cost market access or trigger interest-rate pain.

- **Market Capitalism makes you structurally vulnerable to convoy raiding by a major navy.** Internal market access between non-adjacent states travels by convoy under this law, so an enemy who can sink your convoys severs your domestic economy as well as your overseas one. Don't pick a fight with the world's biggest navy on this law without a continental land base.

- **Match a compromise law to the size of the sector it modifies, not to its ideological flavor.** Collectivizing 5% of an economy is not worth the political cost; commercial agriculture as the agrarian compromise was correct because Cuba's farming was a small share of the economy.
