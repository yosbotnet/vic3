# Phase 5 — Power-bloc expansion and unraveling

**Episodes:** [ep16](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep16.md) · [ep17](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep17.md)

**TL;DR:** Japan finally turns outward, riding Appointed Bureaucrats and a fresh power bloc into protectorate-first conquests of Brunei, Sulu and Lanfang — but loses Bulongan to the "failed to protect" mechanic mid-truce, and the campaign ends abruptly when ~6 million unemployed laborers appear with no buildings able to absorb them, an anomaly the player suspects is post-patch save corruption rather than a strategic failure.

## Setting the stage

By the start of ep16, the long political project of the Great Wave campaign is essentially done. Japan is a recognized Great Power, sits in constitutional monarchy with parties, has the Empire of the Rising Sun power bloc formed under the Sovereign Empire principle, abolished serfdom, swapped in Responsible Government, and has Per Capita Taxation live — but with chronic taxation-capacity shortfall (one snapshot shows ~200/500 capacity used with a -300 deficit). Appointed Bureaucrats is the next law on the docket, explicitly to fix that ceiling. The Tempo Crisis was conceded long ago; the Honorable Restoration and Industrialize Japan journals are closed. What remains is using the political machinery the player built to actually *do* something abroad.

The map is set up for it. Power-projection level 2 reduces infamy, the bloc gives subject income, and SE Asia is full of weak unrecognised targets. Internally the economy is still fragile — paper and iron are the largest *government* line items, construction has been throttled to defend the deficit, and a radical Education-access movement is mid-spiral. Phase 5 is the bet that outward expansion (protectorate income, no construction spend) can pay the bills the domestic economy can't.

## Narrative

Ep16 opens with elections returning an illegitimate government twice in succession, repeatedly stalling the Appointed Bureaucrats vote. The player refuses to favor a party — Industrialists, Intelligentsia and Church have been grouped into one bloc, so every choice fractures the supporting coalition — and instead drifts toward Traditional Free Elections, endorses the Intelligentsia via Fruitful Endorsements to push the vote chance up to 72%, and waits. Public Schools is enacted to defuse an Education-access revolution; Language in the Classroom follows to bank long-term assimilation at the cost of accepting Dainichi radicalism. Behind that political housekeeping, Brunei is bullied into a protectorate with zero fighting (+500 power-bloc income noted), and Sulu is taken via marine naval invasion of the capital despite losing one earlier battle. The episode ends with Appointed Bureaucrats finally passing, Japan at 5th GDP in the world, and Bulungan queued as the next target while infamy sits at zero.

Ep17 begins on momentum and ends on something close to collapse. Bulongan is invaded immediately on war start using mobilized marines, beaten, and made a protectorate — and then, within weeks, breaks free under the "failed to protect" mechanic because the player is simultaneously tied up in another diplomatic play and has no bandwidth to defend the new subject during its truce window. The wasted war is salvaged in part by demoting Brunei from protectorate to puppet (tighter tier, more income, redder on the map) and by accepting Lanfang's peaceful back-down into vassalage. A subsequent attempt to liberate Qing's subject Joseon is aborted at the war-aim stage when liberty desire refuses to climb — "support independence" turns out to only work when LD is already trending up on its own.

The economy is where the unravelling really shows. Bureaucracy keeps oscillating despite repeated Government Administration builds; paper costs consume roughly 60k of 160k income, so every additional GA makes the underlying problem worse. The player trims GAs back in over-built states, accepts low Training Rate to cut army upkeep, and pushes Education Act up because +5% literacy at level 2 wasn't enough to unblock the next law. Companies finally unlock and the obvious Japanese flavour picks — Mitsubishi (paper + iron + shipyard) and Mitsui (level-5 textile mill for clothing/dyes/silk) — are deliberately *not* taken yet because their prerequisites aren't built and burning a company slot on a default would waste the lever.

The end comes without warning. Across roughly ten minutes of late play the player notices the unemployment count climb from ~600k to 6.19M laborers with no buildings able to absorb them. Radicals spike across the entire pop class. The player can't find an in-game mechanism that explains it — construction had been throttled, but not nearly enough to displace 6 million workers — and concludes the most likely cause is post-patch save corruption from the 1.13.x update cycle, since "the game broke down because of updates and patches." The episode ends early with a screenshot and a note that the bug would have to be investigated via console rather than fought through with laws or edicts. Russia is destabilising in the background (lost St. Petersburg event, "Anti-Russian League"), an opening the player flags but never takes. That is where the campaign stops.

## Key decisions

- **Protectorate-first against Brunei, Sulu, Lanfang** — Threaten, accept back-down where possible, fight only when necessary. Alternative was direct conquest (more infamy, no subject income, no power-bloc member). Reasoning: protectorates yield power-bloc income (+500/Brunei), feed the Sovereign Empire principle the bloc was built around, and leave a path to annexation later at lower infamy. Brunei capitulated without combat; Sulu fell after one lost battle and a successful capital invasion. Cite [ep16](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep16.md).

- **Decline to favor any party, drift toward Traditional Free Elections** — With Industrialists + Intelligentsia + Church bundled into one bloc, "Favor party" risked flipping the supporting coalition for Appointed Bureaucrats. Alternative: meddle for the most-aligned party. Reasoning: prior election meddles in ep12 and ep14 had produced illegitimate governments and stalled the law mid-enactment; no-action preserved the coalition's narrow pass chance. Endorsing the Intelligentsia via Fruitful Endorsements lifted vote chance to 72% without the meddle penalty. Cite [ep16](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep16.md).

- **Enact Public Schools (not a higher tier) to defuse the Education-access revolution** — Movement demanded more education; player chose the cheapest law in the direction the movement wanted. Alternative: hold out for Private Schools / higher SoL impact. Reasoning: the movement is satisfied by the *direction* of the law change, not by the optimal tier; cheap defusal beats holding out for a perfect law that won't pass anyway. Movement stalled rather than crushed. Cite [ep16](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep16.md).

- **Mobilize marines + naval-invade Bulongan on war-start** — Marines arrive instantly via fleet, bypassing transit time and the standard -25% naval-invasion penalty. Alternative: line battalions over transport, longer marshalling. Reasoning: marines bypass invasion penalty timer and the marine cap (12) fits the small overseas target. Required splitting the fleet so marines embark without dragging the whole navy off station, and giving the invasion force highest supply priority over the patrol fleet. Capital fell, war won. Cite [ep16](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep16.md), [ep17](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep17.md).

- **Demote Brunei from protectorate to puppet after losing Bulongan** — Salvage move after the "failed to protect" loss. Alternative: leave subject tiers alone, try to re-take Bulongan. Reasoning: tighter subject tier yields more income, the truce window on Bulongan blocked re-entry, and consolidating what remained was the only short-window play available. Cite [ep17](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep17.md).

- **Abort the Joseon liberation play mid-setup** — Support-independence on a Qing subject, intended to flip Joseon and gain a land border for the bigger Qing fight. Alternative: commit influence and wait. Reasoning: liberty desire never climbed during the staging period, so the subject would not actually break free; spending influence on a play that won't resolve is pure loss. Aborted at war-aim stage. Cite [ep17](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep17.md).

- **Defer Mitsubishi and Mitsui company picks until prerequisites land** — Companies unlocked; instead of taking an available default the player held the slot. Alternative: pick now for partial bonuses. Reasoning: Mitsubishi (paper + iron + shipyard) directly addresses the paper-cost pain that is eating 60k of 160k income; Mitsui (level-5 textile mill) hits clothing/dyes/silk. Both need building prerequisites not yet met. Company slots are scarce — optimise for input-goods the economy bleeds on, not raw output bonuses. Cite [ep17](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep17.md).

- **Trim Government Administrations in over-built states once bureaucracy spiked positive** — Reversing the GA-spam pattern. Alternative: keep them for headroom. Reasoning: each GA costs paper; with paper price already crushing the budget every surplus GA was a negative-yield building. Free goods demand by downgrading, then build only where taxation is genuinely bottlenecked (e.g. Shikoku). Cite [ep17](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep17.md).

- **End the episode early to investigate the 6M unemployed-laborers anomaly** — Plan was to screenshot, suspect a patch bug, possibly use console to clear radicals rather than continue play. Alternative: keep playing and try to absorb pops via emergency construction. Reasoning: the unemployment count grew faster than any plausible construction queue could absorb (~600k → 6.19M in ~10 minutes of play); if the underlying cause is save corruption from the 1.13.x patch cycle, no in-game tool addresses it. Don't fight a phantom crisis with mechanics that can't reach it. Cite [ep17](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/great-wave-japan/ep17.md).

## Phase-specific lessons

- **You can't protect a subject while you're tied up in another diplomatic play.** "Failed to protect" auto-independence will wipe a freshly-conquered protectorate during its truce window if you have no diplomatic bandwidth to defend it. Sequence subject-creation wars so the truce period falls in a quiet diplomatic stretch, or accept that overlapping plays will cost you the more recent subject. Bulongan's flip back to independence is the campaign's clearest case.

- **Mass unemployment with no employable buildings is a danger sign — and sometimes an unfixable one.** When pop growth outpaces construction, radicals spiral past any law or edict you can throw at them, and once an entire pop class is mass-radicalised it self-sustains regardless of standard-of-living interventions. The corollary: if the unemployment number is implausibly large (millions in a few in-game weeks), suspect a patch/save bug before suspecting your strategy, screenshot for evidence, and don't burn construction queues fighting a phantom problem.

- **Bureaucracy crises are usually a paper-cost problem, not a building-count problem.** When paper price spikes, every extra Government Administration makes income worse, because each GA's paper consumption pushes the price further. Trim GAs in over-built states, balance via tax-capacity laws (Appointed Bureaucrats), and only build new GAs where taxation is genuinely bottlenecked. The signal isn't bureaucracy capacity, it's the paper-cost line item in your state spending.

- **"Support independence" is conditional on a climbing liberty-desire trend.** The diplomatic play stalls — and your influence spend evaporates — if LD is flat or falling at the moment of commitment. Check the trend before clicking, not just the absolute value. Same family of mistake: you cannot reach a country diplomatically without sufficient interest tier in its strategic region, so expansion plans must include interest-tier prerequisites.

- **Protectorate-first expansion is a viable economic outlet when domestic construction is starving.** It doesn't consume construction points, it adds power-bloc income, and it builds toward later annexation at lower cumulative infamy than direct conquest. The hidden cost is the truce-window protection burden — budget for it explicitly, or expect to lose the most recent subject.

## Campaign retrospective

Across 17 episodes the Great Wave campaign is a piece of *political* engineering that succeeds — Japan is recognized, constitutional, modernizing, and at 5th GDP — but whose economic and military projects always lag the political ones by a phase. The pattern is consistent from the Tempo Crisis concession in ep06 through the Korea abort in ep14 to Bulongan's loss in ep17: when the campaign tries to act on the world (war, expansion, big infrastructure pushes), it discovers that the supporting systems (supply ships, construction queue, subject-protection bandwidth, employable buildings for growing pops) weren't built in time. The political wins (Imperial Restoration, Lex Edo Status routed via Landed Voting, Per Capita Taxation, Appointed Bureaucrats, the Sovereign Empire bloc) are textbook; the operational follow-through is improvised under pressure and frequently bleeds.

If the player were running this campaign again, the high-leverage changes are concrete. Sequence the SE Asia expansion serially with explicit no-overlap truce windows so "failed to protect" cannot fire — Bulongan was conquered while another play was already live, and that single sequencing error wiped a war. Pick Mitsubishi as the first company the moment paper-cost crosses ~30% of state spending rather than waiting on the perfect prerequisite, because the company exists precisely to break the paper price you are already paying. Run a construction-vs-pop-growth dashboard from ep13 onward so the laborer absorption gap is visible before it becomes a 6M radical class. Refuse election meddles entirely in late game — every meddle attempted in this campaign (ep12, ep14, ep16) either produced an illegitimate government or stalled a law mid-enactment, and the no-meddle drift toward Traditional Free Elections in ep16 was the move that actually worked. Finally, treat unexplained anomalies (St. Petersburg event, sudden unemployment spike) as either opportunities or evidence of a save problem on first sight, not as background noise — the campaign repeatedly logged background signals it didn't act on, and the unemployment one is what ended the run.
