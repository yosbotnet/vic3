# Case Study: Belgium — Conquer, Colonize, Reaction

**Source:** [The Comprehensive Victoria 3 Tutorial (2025) | Iberian Twilight](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P_PzglCFo9I) by Tarkusarkusar, 2025-12-16, 2:27:15. Patch unspecified (recorded December 2025; references a free patch shipped "a few days" before the stream that added the law-negotiation button). The video is a full systems tutorial (Economics → Pops → Politics → Diplomacy → War) whose final ~56 minutes are this applied playthrough; the "Iberian Twilight" subtitle names the throwaway demo save used to illustrate mechanics in the lecture half, *not* this campaign. Notes: [06-belgium-synthesis](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/comprehensive-tutorial-2025/06-belgium-synthesis.md).

**TL;DR:** A short, semi-improvised teaching run that takes 1836 Belgium and pursues three deliberately mismatched goals chosen live with two first-time co-hosts: conquer the Netherlands (historical-rival roleplay), colonize the Congo, and — the "spanner" challenge — drag structurally-liberal Belgium into an absolute monarchy. The two war goals fall almost trivially; the political goal is, in the host's own words, "significantly harder than invading the Netherlands and the Congo," and it only resolves by accident when a civil war forces *universal suffrage*, which devalues votes enough to make autocracy passable. The run's value is in the narrated thought process and the failures that are left in: a "big-brain" company built to manufacture authoritarian aristocrats spawns its headquarters in the wrong state and accomplishes nothing. It is the showcase for the video's thesis — every goal in Victoria 3, even a political one, routes through the economy first.

## Setting the stage

Belgium opens in 1836 as a small, rich, structurally liberal Western European state with spare budget, decent military tech, and the geography for an early war against the Netherlands. The host frames it as the best *learning* country precisely because it punishes mistakes loudly — a wrong move gets you conquered or stalls your economy visibly, where an easy country (Sweden, Switzerland, the USA) lets you play badly and never notice [[notes](https://github.com/yosbotnet/vic3/blob/main/notes/comprehensive-tutorial-2025/06-belgium-synthesis.md)].

The goals are set before touching the map and are intentionally cross-grained: a war goal, a colonial goal, and a political goal that runs *against* the country's grain. That last one is the teaching device — authoritarianism is badly suited to 1836 Belgium, so forcing it stress-tests every political lever the lecture half introduced. The given that organizes all three: "everything hinges on the economy." You cannot colonize without demand and construction; you cannot win a war you can't pay for in goods; you cannot flip a country's laws without the pops and characters that only an economy produces.

## Narrative

The economic opening is clean and is the template the rest of the run leans on. Spare budget is dripping uselessly into the gold reserve, so it goes into **four construction sectors**, then **iron mines** to feed them, then **railways** because the iron mines eat infrastructure (buildings consume infrastructure, railways supply it) — the queue reordered so infrastructure arrives early [1:31:30]. Authority is plentiful and free to reclaim, so four decrees go down at once: road maintenance (construction efficiency + infrastructure) where the queue sits, a resource-industry decree on resource-heavy Wallonia (~26% throughput), an agricultural decree on farm-heavy Flanders (doubling as a way to seed aristocrats and conservatives), and enlistment for the coming war [1:32:30]. Tech prioritizes **civilizing mission** for the Congo, but because it is tier 3 the whole tier-2 shelf is queued first rather than waiting on the target alone [1:33:31]. Diplomacy is "be friends with everyone except the target" — relations bought cheaply with France, Prussia, and Britain so none of them join the Netherlands [1:34:01]. Companies are handed as much of the economy as they will take, because more buildings under a company means more throughput overall [1:36:01]. When debt starts to build, the host runs a **"differential diagnosis"** on the budget: ignore the big gold coins, find the input consumed in the highest *volume* (iron, ~4× the real cost of pricier-looking fabric), and lower its price by building more of it [1:38:00].

The two military goals are dispatched with a market trick. Rather than grind the Netherlands war alone, Belgium **becomes a subject of Prussia** to ride its strong, ammunition-rich market — which lets it safely upgrade to skirmish infantry and call Prussia in as a free second army — and the war is won in months [1:44:31]. The host then immediately **sabotages** the Prussian relationship, asking Prussia for things it will refuse so that relations sour and liberty desire climbs back above the 25 threshold below which a subject can be puppeted [1:48:00]. Holland is incorporated (~27 months); the Congo, conquered by a six-ship naval landing, is deliberately *left unincorporated* — incorporating it would take 22 years because the wrong-culture homelands share no heritage or language, and unincorporated land can't be taxed but does pay collapsed wages that become explosive plantation profit [1:46:31][1:55:00]. The fresh conquest's turmoil is suppressed with the **violent-treatment ("chop hands") decree**, trading pop mortality for a smaller turmoil penalty so construction is less crippled while standard of living slowly recovers from market access [1:57:01]. Malaria caps colony growth at −95%, so **malaria-prevention** (tier 4) becomes the research priority, funded by government-only universities [2:02:01].

The political goal is where the run earns its keep by failing. Ideology in Victoria 3 comes from *characters*, and character rolls are "all gambling." The intended method — exile-and-reload an interest-group leader until an authoritarian rolls (6% on the industrialists) — is too slow, so the host tries cheaper hacks: bolstering the reactionary movement to raise authoritarian odds, **rigging elections** to pump the Catholic/Conservative party at the cost of electoral confidence and legitimacy, and installing authoritarian general Alexandru Lahovari to flip the armed-forces interest group's party — which backfires when he forms *his own* party instead [2:00:00][2:04:01]. The cleverest move backfires worst: the **Belgian Plantation company**, picked for +10% colony growth, a coffee prestige good, *and* partial aristocratic ownership meant to manufacture authoritarian-leaning aristocrats, spawns its headquarters inside the unincorporated Congo — so the aristocrats it creates are local Africans with −50% pop political strength who can't be taxed and carry no political weight, and the company later relocates entirely when the colony rebels [2:08:00][2:14:30]. Autocracy finally passes by accident: conceding everything triggers a civil war, the "**reforms at gunpoint**" event forces *universal suffrage*, and because nobody likes universal suffrage it devalues votes enough that autocracy becomes trivial to pass [2:22:01]. The Congo rebellion is crushed, which conveniently kills off its own radicals, and Belgium ends an absolute monarchy with goals one and two (partially) and three achieved [2:23:30].

## Key decisions

- **Set the goal before touching the map — but expect every goal to route through the economy.** The alternative is to start building reactively. Rejected because Victoria 3, unlike CK3, lets you skip almost nothing: standard-of-living goals need jobs need buildings; ideological goals need the pops and clout an economy produces; colonial goals need demand and construction. Pick GDP-max, SoL-max, an ideological target, roleplay, or a challenge run first, then work backward to the economy that enables it [1:30:00]. See [Choosing a Goal & Playstyles](../strategy/choosing-a-goal.md).

- **Open four construction sectors → iron mines → railways, in that dependency order.** The alternative was to let spare budget accumulate in the gold reserve or to build sectors without securing their inputs. Rejected because idle treasury is wasted growth and a construction queue starved of iron or infrastructure stalls. Railways specifically because buildings *consume* infrastructure and railways *supply* it — the iron mines would have hit the cap otherwise [1:31:30][1:32:01].

- **Stack road-maintenance / resource-industry / agricultural / enlistment decrees immediately.** The alternative was to hoard authority. Rejected because early authority is plentiful and decrees are free to reclaim (pull the decree, get the authority back), so a decree left unset is pure waste. Each was placed where it compounded: road maintenance on the build queue, resource-industry on Wallonia, agricultural on Flanders (also seeding conservatives), enlistment ahead of the war [1:32:30][1:34:31].

- **Become a subject of Prussia to win the Netherlands war, then sabotage the relationship.** The alternative was to fight the war independently with home-built conscripts. Rejected because subjecthood hands you the overlord's market — Prussia's was strong and ammunition-rich, enabling skirmish infantry and a free Prussian army — winning the war in months for almost nothing. The downside (being puppeted below 25 liberty desire) is neutralized by *deliberately* asking Prussia for refusable concessions to keep relations low and liberty desire high. Subjecthood is a market cheat with a leash; manage the leash [1:44:31][1:48:00]. See [Small-Nation Play](../diplomacy/small-nation-play.md).

- **Incorporate Holland; leave the Congo unincorporated.** The alternative was to incorporate everything for tax control. Rejected because incorporation costs bureaucracy and time scaled by *cultural distance* — Holland takes ~27 months, the Congo 22 years (a fifth of the game) because its homelands share no heritage or language with Belgium. Incorporate close-culture conquests for taxes; leave distant colonies unincorporated so their collapsed wages flood plantation profit instead [1:46:31][1:55:00]. See [Colonization](../diplomacy/colonization.md).

- **Run plantations on the agricultural decree for pure-profit throughput.** The alternative was to treat plantation throughput like factory throughput (which raises input *costs* alongside output). Rejected because plantations have no inputs except wages, and throughput does not raise wages — so any throughput bonus is straight extra output at no added cost. Pick the plantation crop that matches an available prestige-goods company (here coffee) [2:12:31].

- **Build a company specifically to manufacture a pop class for politics — and watch where its HQ spawns.** The alternative was to pick a company purely on profit. The intent was sound: the Belgian Plantation company's partial aristocratic ownership should have created authoritarian-leaning aristocrats. It failed because the HQ spawned in the unincorporated Congo, where the resulting aristocrats are local Africans at −50% pop political strength who can't be taxed and carry no clout — then the company relocated when the colony rebelled. The lesson is the caveat, not the move: a pop-engineering company is only worth it if its HQ lands somewhere its pops have political weight [2:08:00][2:14:30].

- **Force authoritarianism through characters, accepting that it is mostly gambling.** The alternatives stacked: exile-and-reload leader rolls (too slow at 6%), bolster reactionaries, rig elections (costs legitimacy/electoral confidence), install an authoritarian general to flip a party (he formed his own instead). Reasoning: ideology flows from interest-group *leaders*, whose ideology is a randomized roll nudged by movement pressure — so the only levers are stacking the odds and re-rolling, and every lever has a political cost. Regressing a country's laws is the hardest thing in the game [2:00:00][2:04:01]. See [Passing Laws](../laws-and-politics/passing-laws.md) and [Political Movements](../laws-and-politics/political-movements.md).

- **Let the civil war run rather than suppressing it immediately.** The alternative was to avoid the crisis. Letting the "reforms at gunpoint" event pass *universal suffrage* looked like a defeat — it is the opposite of the goal — but devaluing votes made autocracy trivially passable, and crushing the parallel Congo rebellion cleared its radicals. Sometimes the cheapest path to a law is the disaster you didn't plan [2:22:01][2:23:00].

## Reusable lessons

- **Pick a goal first; every goal routes through the economy.** GDP-max, SoL-max, ideological, roleplay, or challenge — the on-ramp is always jobs, buildings, and demand. You can't pursue a goal whose mechanics you don't understand, so learn the systems, then choose creatively.

- **Conquer the weak, deal with the strong.** War is for what diplomacy can't buy; weak neighbours cost almost nothing to take, while friendships and market deals with strong powers keep you from being the weak one. Be friends with everyone except the actual target so nobody joins their side.

- **Diagnose the budget by consumption × price, not sticker price.** A modestly priced input consumed in huge volume (iron) costs far more than a flashy expensive one consumed rarely (fabric). Lower the highest *total-cost* input by building more of it — a differential diagnosis, not a spending cut.

- **Subjecthood is a market cheat with a leash.** Becoming a subject of a strong, resource-rich market wins wars cheaply and shares the overlord's goods — but stay above 25 liberty desire (deliberately sour relations if you must) or you get puppeted.

- **Incorporate for taxes, stay unincorporated for profit.** Incorporation cost scales with cultural distance — trivial for same-culture states, a fifth of the game for wrong-culture homelands. Tax close conquests; farm distant colonies on collapsed wages.

- **Plantation throughput is pure profit.** No inputs except wages, and throughput doesn't touch wages — so the agricultural decree and a matching prestige-goods company are free output.

- **Forcing reactionary politics on a liberal country is the hardest thing in the game, and it's mostly gambling.** Ideology lives in characters; you can only stack the odds (bolster reactionaries, rig elections, install leaders) and re-roll, and each lever costs legitimacy and frequently backfires.

- **Mechanics can quietly invert your plan — verify where things actually land.** A company built to seed a pop class is useless if its HQ spawns where those pops have no political strength. Check the result, not the intent.

- **Crises can resolve goals by accident — let some of them run.** A civil war forced universal suffrage that made autocracy trivial; a suppressed rebellion cleared its own radicals.
